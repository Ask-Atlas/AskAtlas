THE FUNDAMENTAL THEOREM OF CALCULUS

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, usually called the FTC, is the bridge between the two main operations of calculus: differentiation and integration. It comes in two parts. Together, they say that differentiation and definite integration are inverse operations, in a very precise sense. This is why the notation for antiderivatives and definite integrals shares the integral sign.

PART 1: DIFFERENTIATING AN INTEGRAL

Let f be a continuous function on an interval containing the point a. Define a new function F by

    F(x) = integral from a to x of f(t) dt.

Then F is differentiable, and F’(x) = f(x). In other words, differentiating the accumulated area under f with respect to the upper limit gives you back the original function f evaluated at that upper limit.

A typical MATH 171 application looks like this. If g(x) is the integral from 0 to x of sin(t squared) dt, then even though we cannot write a closed-form antiderivative for sin(t squared), we still know that g’(x) = sin(x squared) exactly. Combined with the chain rule, if the upper limit is a function u(x), then d/dx of the integral from a to u(x) of f(t) dt is f(u(x)) times u’(x).

PART 2: EVALUATING A DEFINITE INTEGRAL

Let f be continuous on the closed interval from a to b, and let F be any antiderivative of f, meaning F’(x) = f(x). Then

    integral from a to b of f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a).

This is the rule that turns the problem of evaluating a definite integral into the much easier problem of finding an antiderivative and subtracting two numbers.

WHY BOTH PARTS MATTER

Part 1 tells us that every continuous function has an antiderivative, namely the accumulation function F(x) defined by integrating from a fixed lower limit to x. This is a theoretical guarantee.

Part 2 tells us how to actually compute a definite integral once we can find an antiderivative. This is the practical tool.

Together, they convert an area problem into a pair of antiderivative evaluations. Without the FTC, every definite integral would require a limit of Riemann sums.

QUICK WORKED EXAMPLES

Example A. Evaluate the integral from 0 to 2 of x squared dx. An antiderivative of x squared is x cubed over 3. By Part 2, the value is 8/3 minus 0 which equals 8/3.

Example B. If h(x) is the integral from 1 to x cubed of e to the t dt, find h’(x). By Part 1 plus the chain rule, h’(x) = e to the x cubed, times 3 x squared.

COMMON PITFALLS

Remember that the FTC requires f to be continuous on the interval of integration. If f has a vertical asymptote inside the interval, you must split the integral and use improper integrals.

Do not forget the negative sign when the lower limit is the variable instead of the upper limit. Swapping limits flips the sign.
