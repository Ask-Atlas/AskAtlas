# Primary Source Analysis Worksheet

Student name: ____________________________  Section: ____________  Date: ____________

Use this worksheet for one primary source assigned in HIST 105. Answer every prompt in complete sentences. The framework draws on Sam Wineburg's Reading Like a Historian (Stanford, 2011), which identifies four habits: **sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, and close reading**.

## Step 1: Identify the Source

- Author or creator: ____________________________
- Title: ____________________________
- Date composed: ____________________________
- Place of composition: ____________________________
- Format (letter, treaty, speech, photograph, court record, etc.): ____________________________
- Intended audience: ____________________________

## Step 2: Sourcing

1. Who is the author, and what was their position at the time of writing? (Two sentences.)
2. What did the author have to gain or lose by producing this document?
3. Is this an eyewitness account, a secondhand report, or a retrospective reflection? How do you know?

## Step 3: Contextualization

Place the document in its moment. Three prompts:

1. Name one major event or process happening in the same country/region within five years of this source (e.g., Treaty of Westphalia 1648, abolition of the Atlantic slave trade 1807, Russian Revolution 1917, Bandung Conference 1955).
2. What technologies of communication were available — manuscript, print, telegraph, radio, internet? How does that shape the source's reach?
3. Identify one contemporary voice (person or institution) that would likely have disagreed with this author, and say why.

## Step 4: Close Reading

Quote two short passages (maximum 25 words each) and annotate them:

- **Passage A:** "__________________________________________________"
  - Key word or phrase: _______________
  - What does this word signal about the author's worldview?

- **Passage B:** "__________________________________________________"
  - Rhetorical device used (metaphor, appeal to authority, statistics, scripture, etc.): _______________
  - Who is the author trying to persuade, and of what?

## Step 5: Corroboration

Identify a second source — primary or secondary — that speaks to the same event or theme.

- Second source citation: ____________________________
- Where do the two sources agree? ____________________________
- Where do they disagree? ____________________________
- Which do you find more credible, and on what grounds?

## Step 6: Synthesis (one paragraph, 120–180 words)

In your own words, explain what this source reveals about its historical moment that a textbook summary would miss. Avoid generic language ("this shows the time period"); be specific about institutions, actors, and stakes. Cite line numbers or page references from the source.
