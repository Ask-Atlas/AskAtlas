# Globalization Timeline: From Silk Road to WTO

Globalization is not a modern invention. Long-distance trade and cultural exchange reshaped societies well before the nineteenth century, but the scale and speed accelerated dramatically after 1500. This timeline identifies eight inflection points that HIST 105 treats as structural turning points.

## Chronology of Integration

| Era | Dates | Inflection Point | Key Actors / Events |
|---|---|---|---|
| Classical | 130 BCE – 1453 CE | Silk Road network | Han Emperor Wu's envoy Zhang Qian (138 BCE); Sogdian, Parthian, and Roman merchants; fall of Constantinople (1453) closes overland routes |
| Medieval | 1095 – 1291 | Crusades & Italian city-states | Venice and Genoa build Mediterranean trade empires; Marco Polo (1271–1295) reaches Khanbaliq |
| Early Modern | 1492 – 1600 | Columbian Exchange begins | Columbus (1492), Vasco da Gama to Calicut (1498), Treaty of Tordesillas (1494); Potosi silver mine opens (1545) |
| Mercantilist | 1600 – 1757 | Chartered trading companies | English East India Company (1600), Dutch VOC (1602); Battle of Plassey (1757) begins Company rule in Bengal |
| Industrial | 1820 – 1914 | First great wave | Suez Canal (1869), transatlantic telegraph (1866), gold standard (1870s); global trade grows 3.5% annually |
| Interwar collapse | 1914 – 1945 | Deglobalization | WWI, Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930), Great Depression, WWII |
| Bretton Woods | 1944 – 1971 | Managed multilateralism | IMF and World Bank (1944), GATT (1947), Marshall Plan (1948), Nixon Shock (1971) |
| Neoliberal | 1989 – present | Hyper-globalization | Fall of Berlin Wall (1989), NAFTA (1994), WTO founded (1995), China WTO accession (2001) |

## Reading the Pattern

Three observations anchor the course argument. First, integration is reversible. The collapse between 1914 and 1945 erased decades of nineteenth-century gains and should correct any assumption that globalization is linear. Second, each wave has been driven by a coupling of technology and institutions: the compass and the joint-stock company in the 1600s; steam, telegraph, and the gold standard in the 1800s; the container ship (introduced 1956 by Malcom McLean) and the WTO rulebook after 1995.

Third, globalization has always produced winners and losers within societies, not only between them. David Ricardo's 1817 theory of comparative advantage predicted aggregate gains, but the Lancashire handloom weavers displaced by Indian cotton imports, the Rust Belt workers after NAFTA, and the Bengali artisans ruined by Company tariffs all suggest the distribution matters as much as the total.
