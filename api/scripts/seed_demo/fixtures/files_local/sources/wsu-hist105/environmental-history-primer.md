# Environmental History Primer

Environmental history, pioneered by scholars such as Alfred Crosby (Ecological Imperialism, 1986) and William Cronon (Changes in the Land, 1983), treats the biosphere as an active agent in human affairs rather than a stage set. HIST 105 uses three episodes to demonstrate the method.

## 1. The Columbian Exchange (after 1492)

Christopher Columbus's 1492 voyage initiated the largest biological reorganization of the Holocene. In both directions the exchange was transformative, but asymmetric.

| Direction | Organisms transferred | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Old World → Americas | Smallpox, measles, influenza; horses, cattle, pigs, wheat | Indigenous population collapse of 70–90% (Noble David Cook, 1998); range-based pastoralism |
| Americas → Old World | Maize, potato, tomato, cassava, tobacco, syphilis | European population rises from ~80M (1500) to ~180M (1800); Irish Potato Famine (1845–1852) kills 1M |

Crosby argued that the Exchange made European expansion demographically possible. Historian Charles Mann (1493, 2011) extended this to a "Homogenocene" era of globalized biota.

## 2. The Industrial Revolution and the Great Acceleration

Britain's transition from biomass to coal, conventionally dated to James Watt's improved steam engine (patented 1769), dissolved the Malthusian ceiling on energy. By 1850 Britain burned 60 million tons of coal a year. Atmospheric CO2, stable near 280 ppm for 10,000 years, began its upward drift.

The "Great Acceleration" (Will Steffen et al., 2004) names the post-1950 surge:

| Indicator | 1950 | 2000 |
|---|---|---|
| Global population | 2.5B | 6.1B |
| Primary energy use | 28 EJ | 420 EJ |
| Atmospheric CO2 | 311 ppm | 369 ppm |
| Large dams | ~5,000 | ~45,000 |

## 3. Climate as Historical Force

Environmental historians increasingly read climate signals into traditional narratives. Geoffrey Parker's Global Crisis (2013) links the Little Ice Age (roughly 1300–1850, coldest during the Maunder Minimum 1645–1715) to the General Crisis of the seventeenth century: the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648), the fall of the Ming dynasty (1644), and the English Civil War (1642–1651). Sam White's The Climate of Rebellion (2011) applies the same frame to the Ottoman Celali revolts of the 1590s.

## Takeaways

Environmental history denies the nature–culture dichotomy. Every political event has an ecological underside: crops that failed, rivers that shifted, epidemics that emptied towns. For HIST 105 the payoff is simple — contemporary issues like migration, food security, and climate policy are legible only when read against five centuries of biological and energetic change.
