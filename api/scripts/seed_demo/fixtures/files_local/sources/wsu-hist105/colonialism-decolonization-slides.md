# Colonialism and Decolonization

## Slide 1 — Title

HIST 105 lecture deck. Five centuries from the Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) to Hong Kong's handover (1997).

## Slide 2 — Three Waves of European Expansion

Iberian (1492–1640), Dutch/English/French (1600–1815), New Imperialism (1870–1914).

## Slide 3 — The First Wave: Iberia

Columbus (1492), Treaty of Tordesillas (1494), Magellan–Elcano circumnavigation (1519–1522), Cortés conquers Tenochtitlan (1521), Pizarro captures Atahualpa (1532).

## Slide 4 — Silver and the Price Revolution

Potosí opens 1545. By 1600, 40% of world silver flows through Spain. Earl J. Hamilton's data shows European prices quintupling 1500–1650.

## Slide 5 — The Atlantic Slave Trade

~12.5 million Africans embarked between 1501 and 1867 (Slave Voyages database). Middle Passage mortality ~15%. Abolition: Britain 1807, United States 1808, Brazil 1888.

## Slide 6 — Chartered Companies

English East India Company (1600), Dutch VOC (1602). VOC paid 18% annual dividends 1602–1796. Battle of Plassey (1757) transfers Bengal revenue to the EIC.

## Slide 7 — Enlightenment Critiques

Bartolomé de las Casas (A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies, 1542), Denis Diderot's contributions to Raynal's History of the Two Indies (1770), Adam Smith's critique of mercantilism in Wealth of Nations (1776).

## Slide 8 — Haitian Revolution (1791–1804)

Toussaint Louverture; defeats British, Spanish, and Napoleonic forces. Haiti declared independent 1 January 1804 — the second republic in the Americas and the only successful large-scale slave revolt.

## Slide 9 — New Imperialism

Berlin Conference 1884–1885 partitions Africa. By 1914, Europe controls 84% of the globe (Daniel Headrick, The Tools of Empire, 1981). Drivers: quinine, steamships, Maxim gun, telegraph.

## Slide 10 — Scramble for Africa

Leopold II's Congo Free State (1885–1908) — ~10 million deaths (Adam Hochschild, King Leopold's Ghost, 1998). Fashoda crisis 1898. South African War 1899–1902.

## Slide 11 — Asian Colonies

British Raj (1858–1947), French Indochina (1887–1954), Dutch East Indies (to 1949), American Philippines (1898–1946).

## Slide 12 — Resistance Before 1945

Indian Rebellion (1857), Boxer Uprising (1899–1901), Herero and Nama genocide (1904–1908), Easter Rising in Dublin (1916), Amritsar massacre (1919).

## Slide 13 — World Wars as Solvent

Colonial troops: 1.3M Indians in WWI, 2.5M in WWII. Japan's 1942 conquests shatter European prestige in Asia. The Atlantic Charter (1941) promises self-determination.

## Slide 14 — 1947: India and Pakistan

Partition displaces 14M; ~1M die. Gandhi assassinated 30 January 1948.

## Slide 15 — Bandung and the Third World

Bandung Conference 1955: 29 Asian and African states meet in Indonesia. Sukarno, Nehru, Zhou Enlai, Nasser. Non-Aligned Movement founded 1961.

## Slide 16 — Africa's Year: 1960

Seventeen African states gain independence in 1960, including Nigeria, Senegal, and the Congo. Patrice Lumumba assassinated January 1961.

## Slide 17 — Violent Decolonizations

Algerian War (1954–1962), Kenyan Mau Mau (1952–1960), Vietnamese wars against France (to 1954) and the US (to 1975), Angolan and Mozambican wars (to 1975).

## Slide 18 — Late and Lingering

Portuguese empire ends 1974–1975 after the Carnation Revolution. Zimbabwe 1980. Namibia 1990. Hong Kong 1997. Macau 1999.

## Slide 19 — Legacies

Arbitrary borders, extractive institutions (Acemoglu–Robinson, 2001), language hierarchies, diaspora communities, ongoing debates over restitution and reparations.

## Slide 20 — Discussion Questions

Was decolonization completed in 1997, or is it ongoing? What role did the Cold War play in accelerating or complicating independence movements?
