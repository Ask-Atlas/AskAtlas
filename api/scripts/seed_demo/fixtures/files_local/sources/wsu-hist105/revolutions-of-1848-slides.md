# The Revolutions of 1848 and the Rise of Nationalism

## Slide 1 — Title

HIST 105 lecture deck. A single year — 1848 — saw uprisings in more than fifty European cities. They almost all failed. Their consequences shaped the next seventy years.

## Slide 2 — The Big Picture

The old order set by the Congress of Vienna (1815) cracked in a matter of weeks: February in Paris, March in Vienna and Berlin, Milan, Venice, Budapest, Prague, Rome. By late 1849, monarchs had reasserted control almost everywhere — but liberalism and nationalism had become mass movements.

## Slide 3 — Pre-1848 Pressures

- **Hunger**: 1845–1847 potato blight and grain failures. Irish Famine spreads anxiety westward.
- **Industrial dislocation**: railway boom collapse 1847, banking panic.
- **Political exclusion**: middle classes shut out of legislatures; guilds squeezed by free trade.
- **Ideological ferment**: Mill, Marx & Engels (*Communist Manifesto*, February 1848), Mazzini.

## Slide 4 — February: Paris

King Louis-Philippe abdicates 24 February. A provisional republic proclaims universal male suffrage (9M voters overnight), national workshops (Louis Blanc), and abolishes slavery in French colonies (27 April 1848).

## Slide 5 — March: Vienna and Berlin

Metternich — architect of the Vienna system — flees Austria on 13 March. Frederick William IV of Prussia promises a constitution on 18 March after Berlin street fighting. The Habsburg empire looks like it might dissolve.

## Slide 6 — The Italian Peninsula

Milan's Five Days (18–22 March) expel Austrian troops. Venice proclaims the Republic of San Marco. Pope Pius IX flees Rome in November; a Roman Republic is declared in February 1849 with Mazzini at its head.

## Slide 7 — The Frankfurt Parliament

A pan-German assembly of 586 delegates meets in St. Paul's Church from 18 May 1848. It drafts a liberal constitution and offers the imperial crown to Frederick William IV. He refuses it in April 1849 — a "crown from the gutter."

## Slide 8 — Hungary: Kossuth's Revolution

Lajos Kossuth leads the Hungarian Diet to declare independence in April 1849. Austria calls in Russian troops (Tsar Nicholas I obliges). Hungary surrenders at Világos, 13 August 1849.

## Slide 9 — The June Days in Paris

Closure of the national workshops triggers a workers' insurrection 23–26 June 1848. General Cavaignac crushes it: ~4,000 dead, 12,000 deported. A key turning point — liberals side with order over the workers.

## Slide 10 — Why the Revolutions Failed

- Split between liberal bourgeois and socialist working-class goals.
- Nationalist movements conflicted with each other (Czech vs. German, Hungarian vs. Slavic).
- Armies stayed loyal to monarchs.
- No coordinated leadership across borders.
- Peasant demands (end of feudal dues) satisfied early, removing rural support.

## Slide 11 — The Reaction

By 1852 Louis-Napoleon makes himself Emperor Napoleon III. Franz Joseph imposes neo-absolutism in Austria. Prussia keeps a constitution but with a three-class voting system weighted by tax payment.

## Slide 12 — What Survived

- Serfdom abolished in the Habsburg lands (September 1848) — a change that stuck.
- Universal male suffrage in France (reinstated 1871).
- Jewish emancipation advanced in several German states.
- A generation of exiled revolutionaries (Marx in London, Kossuth in the US, Garibaldi in New York).

## Slide 13 — The Nationalism Pivot

Before 1848, nationalism was largely a liberal cause. After 1848, it splits: liberal nationalism (Mazzini) versus *realpolitik* nationalism (Bismarck, Cavour) that uses the state and the army rather than the barricade.

## Slide 14 — Italian Unification (1859–1870)

Cavour allies with France; victories at Magenta and Solferino (1859). Garibaldi's Thousand land in Sicily (1860). Kingdom of Italy proclaimed 17 March 1861. Rome incorporated 1870.

## Slide 15 — German Unification (1864–1871)

Bismarck engineers three wars: Denmark (1864), Austria (1866), France (1870–71). German Empire proclaimed at Versailles 18 January 1871 — unification from above, on monarchical terms.

## Slide 16 — Long Shadow

1848 was called the "turning point at which modern history failed to turn" (A.J.P. Taylor). The liberal constitutional moment lost; the national state won. Both legacies run through 1914.

## Slide 17 — Discussion Questions

Were the revolutions of 1848 failures or preconditions for later change? Why did nationalism outlast liberalism as a mobilizing force in nineteenth-century Europe?
