Periodization Guide for HIST 105

Periodization is the act of slicing the past into named chunks. It is not a neutral exercise. Every period boundary embeds an argument about what mattered, whose story drives the narrative, and what counts as change versus continuity. This guide introduces four conventions you will meet in the course and explains the stakes of each.

Convention 1: The Western schoolbook model.
Ancient (to 476 CE, the deposition of Romulus Augustulus), Medieval (476 to 1453, the fall of Constantinople), Early Modern (1453 to 1789), Modern (1789 to 1945), Contemporary (1945 to present). The strengths are memorability and coherence with European archival categories. The weaknesses are severe: these dates mean little for Chinese, Islamic, or Amerindian historiography. The Ming dynasty (1368 to 1644) runs across three of these buckets without noticing them.

Convention 2: Dynastic or regnal periodization.
China is conventionally divided by dynasty (Han 202 BCE to 220 CE; Tang 618 to 907; Song 960 to 1279; Yuan 1271 to 1368; Ming 1368 to 1644; Qing 1644 to 1912). Britain uses monarchs (Tudor 1485 to 1603; Stuart 1603 to 1714; Victorian 1837 to 1901). These schemes are administratively precise but treat the death of a ruler as more significant than a famine, a crop failure, or a currency collapse.

Convention 3: Economic and technological eras.
Neolithic (from roughly 10,000 BCE), Bronze Age (from roughly 3300 BCE), Iron Age (from roughly 1200 BCE), Mercantile (1500 to 1750), Industrial (1750 to 1914), Fordist (1914 to 1973), Post-Fordist or neoliberal (1973 to present). The Marxist tradition, following Eric Hobsbawm's Age of Revolution, Age of Capital, Age of Empire, and Age of Extremes, identifies a long nineteenth century (1789 to 1914) and a short twentieth century (1914 to 1991). These periods center production and class rather than crowns.

Convention 4: Environmental or planetary periodization.
Geologists and historians increasingly use Holocene (11,700 years ago to present) and Anthropocene (proposed start dates range from 1610, the Orbis Spike of New World plant exchange identified by Simon Lewis and Mark Maslin in 2015, to 1945, the first nuclear tests, to 1950, the Great Acceleration). This frame asks when human activity began to leave a lasting geological signal.

How to use this guide.
When a textbook names a period, ask three questions. First, whose experience does this label center? Second, what event marks the boundary, and why that one rather than another a decade earlier or later? Third, what continuities does the boundary hide? A Chinese peasant in 1450 and one in 1550 lived similar lives despite straddling the Early Modern line; a Congolese villager's world changed catastrophically between 1880 and 1890 with no schoolbook boundary to mark it. Good history notices when the official clock and the lived clock diverge.
