# Emotion and Motivation

## Slide 1 — Overview

Today: four theories of emotion and two theories of motivation. Goal: know the **order of events** each theory posits and the **evidence** that distinguishes them.

## Slide 2 — What Is an Emotion?

Three components, per Paul Ekman (1992):
- Physiological arousal (heart rate, adrenaline)
- Expressive behavior (facial expression, posture)
- Conscious experience (the feeling)

Theories disagree on which comes first.

## Slide 3 — James-Lange Theory (1884 / 1885)

William James (*What is an emotion?*, 1884) and Carl Lange independently: **we feel afraid because we run**, not the reverse.

Order: **Stimulus → Bodily response → Emotion.**

Prediction: distinct emotions should have distinct bodily signatures.

## Slide 4 — Cannon-Bard Theory (1927)

Walter Cannon and Philip Bard objected: bodily responses are too slow and too similar across emotions to cause them.

Order: **Stimulus → Thalamus simultaneously triggers body and cortex → Arousal and feeling together.**

## Slide 5 — Evidence for Cannon-Bard

- Hohmann (1966): spinal-cord-injury patients still reported emotion, though altered — arousal alone isn't required for feeling.
- Cannon's own work showed epinephrine injections produce arousal without a specific emotion.

## Slide 6 — Schachter-Singer Two-Factor Theory (1962)

Stanley Schachter & Jerome Singer: emotion = **arousal + cognitive label**.

Order: **Stimulus → Arousal → Label based on context → Emotion.**

Classic study: epinephrine-injected participants reported anger or euphoria depending on a confederate's behavior.

## Slide 7 — Dutton & Aron Bridge Study (1974)

Men crossing a shaky suspension bridge rated an interviewer as more attractive than men on a stable bridge — **misattribution of arousal**, supporting Schachter-Singer.

## Slide 8 — Lazarus's Appraisal Theory (1966 / 1991)

Richard Lazarus: cognitive **appraisal** precedes emotion. The same event (failing a test) produces different emotions depending on whether it is appraised as threat, challenge, or loss.

## Slide 9 — Theory Comparison Table

| Theory | Order of Events | Signature Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| James-Lange | Body → Feeling | Facial-feedback (Strack 1988) |
| Cannon-Bard | Body and Feeling together | Spinal injury (Hohmann 1966) |
| Schachter-Singer | Body + Label → Feeling | Epinephrine study (1962) |
| Lazarus Appraisal | Appraisal → Feeling | Stress-response studies |

## Slide 10 — Pivot: Motivation

Motivation = the forces that *initiate, direct, and sustain* behavior. Two big frameworks below.

## Slide 11 — Drive-Reduction Theory

Clark Hull (1943): physiological needs create drives; behavior reduces drives and returns the organism to homeostasis.

Limitation: cannot explain curiosity, play, or why we eat when already full.

## Slide 12 — Yerkes-Dodson Law (1908)

Performance is an **inverted-U function of arousal**: too little → disengagement; too much → choking. Task complexity shifts the optimum leftward.

## Slide 13 — Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

Abraham Maslow proposed a pyramid:

1. Physiological (food, water)
2. Safety
3. Belongingness & love
4. Esteem
5. Self-actualization

Higher needs activate only once lower ones are satisfied. Later editions added cognitive, aesthetic, and transcendence needs.

## Slide 14 — Critiques of Maslow

- Order is not strictly sequential (Wahba & Bridwell, 1976).
- Culturally biased toward individualistic societies.
- Still useful as a heuristic in organizational psychology.

## Slide 15 — Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985)

Three innate needs: **autonomy, competence, relatedness**. Intrinsic motivation requires all three. Overjustification effect (Lepper 1973) shows extrinsic rewards can *undermine* intrinsic motivation.

## Slide 16 — Takeaways

- No single theory wins; each captures part of the picture.
- Emotion is physiological *and* cognitive.
- Motivation is needs-based *and* contextual.
- On the midterm: match theory → prediction → evidence.
