# Perception and Illusions: How the Brain Builds Reality

Perception is not a recording of the world—it is a *construction*. The visual system receives a two-dimensional, upside-down, blurry retinal image and produces a stable three-dimensional scene in real time. The illusions below are not failures of that system; they are windows into the rules it normally follows.

## 1. Gestalt Principles of Grouping

Max Wertheimer, Kurt Koffka, and Wolfgang Köhler founded Gestalt psychology in 1910s Berlin. Their core claim: "the whole is *other than* the sum of its parts." The grouping principles describe how the visual system parses scenes into objects.

| Principle | Rule | Everyday Example |
|---|---|---|
| Proximity | Close items group together | Columns in a table feel like units |
| Similarity | Similar items group together | Same-colored dots form a row |
| Good continuation | Smooth contours are preferred | Two crossing lines, not four angles |
| Closure | The mind completes gaps | IBM logo read as solid letters |
| Common fate | Items moving together group | A flock of birds read as one object |
| Figure/ground | One region is foreground, one background | Rubin's vase–face (1915) |

Wertheimer's (1912) **phi phenomenon**—apparent motion from two blinking lights—was the empirical seed of the whole movement.

## 2. Depth Cues

A 2D retina cannot directly measure distance, so the brain combines many cues.

**Monocular (pictorial):** linear perspective, relative size, texture gradient (J. J. Gibson, 1950), interposition, aerial perspective, height in visual field, and motion parallax.

**Binocular:** convergence of the eyes and **retinal disparity**, the slight offset between the two eyes' images. Bela Julesz's (1960) random-dot stereograms proved disparity alone—without any monocular content—is sufficient for depth.

## 3. Classic Illusions and What They Teach

- **Müller-Lyer (1889)**: two equal lines appear unequal depending on arrowhead orientation. Richard Gregory's size-constancy account (1963) argues the arrows cue the brain to interpret the lines as inside vs. outside corners at different distances.
- **Ponzo illusion (1911)**: converging "railroad tracks" make an upper bar look larger, again implicating depth scaling.
- **Ames room (1946)**: a trapezoidal room viewed through a peephole makes equally-sized people look like a giant and a dwarf—because the brain assumes the room is rectangular.
- **Kanizsa triangle (1955)**: illusory contours appear where none exist, demonstrating closure at work.
- **Rubin vase (1915)**: a bistable figure/ground stimulus; attention flips the interpretation.
- **McGurk effect (McGurk & MacDonald, 1976)**: seeing lips say /ga/ while hearing /ba/ yields the percept /da/—perception is multimodal.

## 4. The Takeaway

Helmholtz (1867) called perception "unconscious inference": the brain makes its best guess given noisy data and built-in priors. Illusions are the cases where those priors are wrong—and therefore the places where the underlying inference machinery becomes visible. Expect to return to this "inference" framing when we cover cognition and decision making.
