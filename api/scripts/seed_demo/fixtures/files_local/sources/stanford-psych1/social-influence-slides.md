# Social Influence and Conformity

## Slide 1 — Overview

Today: why ordinary people do things they believe are wrong. Three classic studies, three modern critiques, one practical takeaway.

## Slide 2 — Three Forms of Social Influence

- **Conformity**: matching attitudes or behavior to a group.
- **Compliance**: changing behavior in response to a request.
- **Obedience**: changing behavior in response to a command from an authority.

They differ in where the pressure comes from — peers, requests, or commanded authority — and in which levers work on them.

## Slide 3 — Asch's Line Experiments (1951, 1955)

Solomon Asch showed subjects a standard line and three comparison lines. Confederates gave unanimous wrong answers on 12 of 18 trials. **~75%** of real subjects conformed at least once; across all critical trials, the conformity rate was ~37%.

## Slide 4 — Why People Conformed in Asch

Post-hoc interviews revealed two motives:

- **Normative influence**: wanting to fit in / avoid rejection.
- **Informational influence**: assuming the group knows something you don't.

Conformity dropped to ~5% when even a single confederate broke ranks — social support matters more than group size past ~3 members.

## Slide 5 — Milgram's Obedience Experiments (1961–1963)

Stanley Milgram at Yale. Subjects ("teachers") administered what they believed were increasing electric shocks to a "learner" (confederate) for wrong answers. 65% went to the maximum 450 volts despite hearing screams and pleas.

## Slide 6 — Milgram Variations

The 65% number is the headline. Milgram ran 18 conditions:

- Learner in same room: obedience drops to 40%.
- Two defiant peer confederates: drops to 10%.
- Authority by phone (not in room): drops to 21%.
- Ordinary-person authority (no lab coat): drops to 20%.

**Situation, not disposition, drove the effect.**

## Slide 7 — Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (1971)

Philip Zimbardo randomly assigned 24 students to "guard" or "prisoner" roles in a basement simulated prison. The study was terminated after 6 days (planned for 14) as guards became abusive.

## Slide 8 — Critiques of the SPE

Re-analysis (Le Texier 2019, Haslam & Reicher 2019) revealed that guards were **explicitly coached** to be tough; prisoners varied widely in their reactions; the drama may have been more performance than emergence. The takeaway today: leadership and framing matter, not "evil roles unleash evil."

## Slide 9 — Milgram Revisited

Burger (2009) ran a partial replication capped at 150V: 70% obedience — essentially unchanged. But modern analysts (Gina Perry, 2013) note Milgram's own data were selectively reported, some subjects were visibly skeptical the shocks were real, and experimenter prods varied by session.

## Slide 10 — Informational Influence in Real Life

Sherif's autokinetic effect (1935): in a dark room, a stationary pinpoint of light appears to move. In groups, individuals converged on a common illusory-motion estimate — and kept it even when tested alone later. **Norms persist beyond the situation that created them.**

## Slide 11 — Minority Influence

Serge Moscovici showed that **consistent minorities** can shift majority views, especially when the minority seems principled rather than rigid. The jury-room scene in *12 Angry Men* is the textbook example.

## Slide 12 — The Bystander Effect

Darley & Latané (1968) after the Kitty Genovese case: the probability *any* individual intervenes decreases as group size grows — diffusion of responsibility. Five-step model: notice → interpret as emergency → assume responsibility → know what to do → act.

## Slide 13 — Persuasion: Cialdini's Six Principles

Robert Cialdini's *Influence* (1984): **reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity.** Each is a legitimate heuristic most of the time; each is exploitable.

## Slide 14 — Foot-in-the-Door and Door-in-the-Face

- **Foot-in-the-door** (Freedman & Fraser 1966): small request → larger request. Works via consistency pressure.
- **Door-in-the-face** (Cialdini et al. 1975): big unreasonable request → real request. Works via reciprocity and contrast.

## Slide 15 — The Practical Takeaway

Three defenses against manipulation:

1. Notice the pressure (name the technique).
2. Slow down — heuristics exploit speed.
3. Seek a dissenting voice, internal or external. One peer breaks Asch; one protestor breaks Milgram.

## Slide 16 — Discussion Questions

Which situation in your own life most resembled Milgram's setup? What one change would have made you more likely to refuse? Does the SPE re-analysis change what the study *teaches*, even if the data are weaker than advertised?
