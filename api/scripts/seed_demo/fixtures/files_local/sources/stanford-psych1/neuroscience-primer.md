# Neuroscience Primer: From Neuron to Signal

This primer reviews the cellular machinery that underlies every psychological process we will study this quarter. You cannot understand perception, learning, or emotion without first understanding the neuron.

## 1. Neuron Anatomy

A typical neuron has four functional regions, first mapped in detail by Santiago Ramón y Cajal (1888), whose Golgi-stained drawings established the **neuron doctrine**—the idea that the brain is built from discrete cells rather than a continuous net.

- **Dendrites** receive chemical signals from upstream neurons.
- **Soma** (cell body) integrates those inputs.
- **Axon** conducts the electrical signal away from the soma; in many vertebrate neurons, it is wrapped in a **myelin sheath** produced by oligodendrocytes (CNS) or Schwann cells (PNS).
- **Axon terminals** release neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft.

Gaps in the myelin called **nodes of Ranvier** enable saltatory conduction, increasing propagation speed up to roughly 120 m/s in large myelinated axons.

## 2. The Action Potential

At rest, a neuron sits near −70 mV, maintained by the sodium–potassium pump and selective K⁺ leak channels. Hodgkin and Huxley's 1952 squid-giant-axon experiments (Nobel 1963) established the now-canonical sequence:

1. **Depolarization** past roughly −55 mV opens voltage-gated Na⁺ channels; Na⁺ rushes in.
2. The membrane potential shoots to ~+40 mV.
3. Na⁺ channels inactivate; voltage-gated K⁺ channels open, driving **repolarization**.
4. A brief **hyperpolarization** follows before the pump restores rest.

The action potential is **all-or-none**: stimulus intensity is encoded by firing rate, not spike amplitude.

## 3. Synaptic Transmission

Otto Loewi's 1921 frog-heart experiment identified acetylcholine as the first known neurotransmitter, confirming chemical (rather than purely electrical) synapses. At a chemical synapse, Ca²⁺ influx triggers vesicle fusion; transmitter binds postsynaptic receptors; the signal is terminated by reuptake, enzymatic degradation, or diffusion.

## 4. Major Neurotransmitter Systems

| Transmitter | Primary Nuclei | Behavioral Role | Classic Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dopamine | VTA, substantia nigra | Reward prediction, motor control | Schultz (1997): VTA neurons fire to unpredicted reward |
| Serotonin (5-HT) | Raphe nuclei | Mood, satiety, sleep | SSRIs (fluoxetine, 1987) treat depression |
| Norepinephrine | Locus coeruleus | Arousal, vigilance | Aston-Jones & Cohen (2005) adaptive-gain theory |
| Acetylcholine | Nucleus basalis | Attention, memory | Loss in Alzheimer's (Bartus, 1982) |
| GABA | Widespread interneurons | Primary inhibitory | Benzodiazepines potentiate GABA-A |
| Glutamate | Cortex, hippocampus | Primary excitatory; LTP | Bliss & Lømo (1973) discovered LTP in rabbit hippocampus |

## 5. Why This Matters for Psychology

Every topic later in the course—Pavlovian conditioning, visual perception, stress, schizophrenia—reduces in part to the chemistry above. When we discuss Kandel's Nobel-winning *Aplysia* work on habituation, for example, the mechanism is synaptic: fewer vesicles released per action potential.
