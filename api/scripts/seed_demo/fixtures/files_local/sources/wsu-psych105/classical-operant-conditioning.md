# Classical and Operant Conditioning Primer

Behavioral psychology studies learning through observable stimulus-response associations. Two paradigms dominate: **classical conditioning** (Pavlov, Watson) and **operant conditioning** (Thorndike, Skinner).

## Classical Conditioning (Pavlov, 1903)

Learning by association between two stimuli. A neutral stimulus comes to elicit a reflexive response after repeated pairing with an unconditioned stimulus.

**Core terms:**

- **Unconditioned Stimulus (US)**: triggers response naturally (food)
- **Unconditioned Response (UR)**: automatic reaction (salivation)
- **Conditioned Stimulus (CS)**: formerly neutral stimulus (bell)
- **Conditioned Response (CR)**: learned reaction to CS (salivation to bell)

**Key processes:**

- **Acquisition** — CS and US paired; CR strengthens
- **Extinction** — CS presented alone; CR weakens
- **Spontaneous recovery** — extinguished CR reappears after a rest period
- **Generalization** — similar stimuli evoke the CR (Little Albert — Watson & Rayner, 1920)
- **Discrimination** — organism distinguishes CS from similar stimuli
- **Higher-order conditioning** — a new CS is paired with an established CS

## Operant Conditioning (Skinner, 1938)

Learning in which voluntary behavior is strengthened or weakened by its consequences. Built on Thorndike's **Law of Effect** (1898): behaviors followed by satisfying consequences are repeated.

### The Four Consequences

| Consequence | Effect on Behavior | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Positive reinforcement | Increases (add pleasant stimulus) | Praise for completing homework |
| Negative reinforcement | Increases (remove aversive stimulus) | Buckling seatbelt stops beeping |
| Positive punishment | Decreases (add aversive stimulus) | Receiving a speeding ticket |
| Negative punishment | Decreases (remove pleasant stimulus) | Losing phone privileges |

"Positive" means *adding* a stimulus; "negative" means *removing* one. It does not mean good or bad.

### Schedules of Reinforcement

Skinner's schedules describe *when* reinforcers are delivered. Partial reinforcement is more resistant to extinction than continuous reinforcement (**partial reinforcement effect**).

| Schedule | Rule | Response Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed Ratio (FR) | After N responses | High rate, brief post-reinforcement pause | Factory piecework |
| Variable Ratio (VR) | After average of N responses | Highest, steadiest rate; extinction-resistant | Slot machines |
| Fixed Interval (FI) | First response after N seconds | Scalloped pattern | Checking mail near delivery time |
| Variable Interval (VI) | First response after average N seconds | Slow, steady rate | Checking for text messages |

### Shaping and Chaining

- **Shaping**: reinforcing successive approximations to a target behavior
- **Chaining**: linking discrete behaviors into a sequence
- **Primary reinforcers**: biologically intrinsic (food, water)
- **Secondary reinforcers**: learned (money, tokens, grades)

## Observational Learning (Bandura, 1961)

Bandura's **Bobo doll experiment** demonstrated that learning can occur through observation without direct reinforcement. Four processes are required: **attention, retention, reproduction, motivation**.

## Biological and Cognitive Limits

Learning is not purely associative. **Taste aversion** (Garcia effect) shows biological preparedness — rats associate nausea with taste but not shock. Tolman's **latent learning** and **cognitive maps** show that internal representations form without reinforcement.
