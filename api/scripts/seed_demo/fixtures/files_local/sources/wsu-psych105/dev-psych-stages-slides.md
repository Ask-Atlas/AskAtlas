# Developmental Psychology: Three Stage Theories

## Slide 1 — Overview

Developmental psychology asks how humans change across the lifespan. Three major stage theorists — **Jean Piaget**, **Erik Erikson**, and **Lawrence Kohlberg** — each proposed distinct but complementary sequences of development.

## Slide 2 — Piaget: Cognitive Development

Piaget (1936) argued children actively construct knowledge through **schemas**, modified by **assimilation** (fitting new info into existing schemas) and **accommodation** (revising schemas for new info). Disequilibrium drives learning.

## Slide 3 — Piaget's Four Stages

| Stage | Age | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Sensorimotor | 0–2 | Learns via senses; **object permanence** emerges ~8 months |
| Preoperational | 2–7 | Symbolic thought; egocentrism; lacks **conservation** |
| Concrete Operational | 7–11 | Logical operations on concrete objects; conservation achieved |
| Formal Operational | 12+ | Abstract and hypothetical reasoning |

## Slide 4 — Piaget: Key Concepts

- **Conservation**: understanding that quantity persists despite changes in appearance
- **Egocentrism**: inability to take another's perspective (three-mountains task)
- **Theory of mind**: develops around age 4 (false-belief task)
- **Critiques**: stage transitions are more gradual and domain-specific than Piaget claimed

## Slide 5 — Erikson: Psychosocial Development

Erikson (1950) expanded Freud's theory to span the entire lifespan. Each stage presents a **psychosocial crisis**: success yields a virtue; failure produces maladjustment.

## Slide 6 — Erikson's Eight Stages

| Stage | Age | Crisis | Virtue |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0–1.5 | Trust vs. Mistrust | Hope |
| 2 | 1.5–3 | Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt | Will |
| 3 | 3–5 | Initiative vs. Guilt | Purpose |
| 4 | 5–12 | Industry vs. Inferiority | Competence |
| 5 | 12–18 | Identity vs. Role Confusion | Fidelity |
| 6 | 18–40 | Intimacy vs. Isolation | Love |
| 7 | 40–65 | Generativity vs. Stagnation | Care |
| 8 | 65+ | Integrity vs. Despair | Wisdom |

## Slide 7 — Erikson: Identity Stage

Adolescence (Stage 5) is pivotal. Marcia (1966) expanded this stage into four **identity statuses**: diffusion, foreclosure, moratorium, and achievement — based on whether a person has explored and committed.

## Slide 8 — Kohlberg: Moral Development

Kohlberg (1958) studied moral reasoning using dilemmas like the **Heinz dilemma** (steal to save spouse?). He proposed three levels with two stages each.

## Slide 9 — Kohlberg's Three Levels

| Level | Stages | Reasoning Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Preconventional | 1. Punishment-obedience; 2. Instrumental exchange | Self-interest, consequences |
| Conventional | 3. Good boy/girl; 4. Law and order | Social approval, rules |
| Postconventional | 5. Social contract; 6. Universal principles | Abstract ethics |

## Slide 10 — Kohlberg: Critiques

- **Gilligan (1982)**: Kohlberg's model favors a male "justice orientation" over a female "care orientation"
- **Cultural bias**: postconventional reasoning reflects Western individualist values
- **Reasoning vs. behavior**: knowing what is right does not guarantee acting rightly

## Slide 11 — Comparing All Three

All three theories are **stage theories**: invariant, universal, and hierarchical sequences. Piaget focuses on **cognition**, Erikson on **social-emotional** growth, and Kohlberg on **moral reasoning**. They complement rather than compete.

## Slide 12 — Modern Extensions

- **Vygotsky**: social and cultural context shapes cognition via the **zone of proximal development**
- **Information-processing theorists**: gradual, continuous change rather than stages
- **Life-span developmentalists** (Baltes): development continues with gains and losses throughout life

## Slide 13 — Exam Tips

Know (a) the order of stages, (b) the defining crisis or task, and (c) one classic experiment for each theorist. Be ready to apply stages to a short case vignette.
