## Background

Tversky and Kahneman (1974) proposed that humans rely on **heuristics** — mental shortcuts — when making judgments under uncertainty. These shortcuts are efficient but produce systematic errors called **cognitive biases**. Kahneman's (2011) dual-process framework distinguishes **System 1** (fast, intuitive, automatic) from **System 2** (slow, deliberate, analytical). Most biases arise when System 1 runs unchecked.

## Reference: Common Biases and Heuristics

| Bias / Heuristic | Definition | Example Cue |
|---|---|---|
| Availability heuristic | Judging likelihood by ease of recall | Overestimating plane crash risk after news coverage |
| Representativeness | Judging likelihood by similarity to prototype | "Linda the bank teller" conjunction fallacy |
| Anchoring | Relying too heavily on first number presented | Sticker price influences negotiation |
| Confirmation bias | Seeking evidence that supports prior beliefs | Only following news that matches your views |
| Hindsight bias | "I knew it all along" after the fact | "Obvious" election outcome in retrospect |
| Fundamental attribution error | Overweight disposition, underweight situation | "He's rude" vs. "he's having a bad day" |
| Self-serving bias | Credit successes internally, blame failures externally | "I aced it" vs. "the test was unfair" |
| Sunk cost fallacy | Continuing due to past investment | Staying in a bad movie because you paid |
| Dunning-Kruger effect | Low skill correlates with overconfidence | Novice coder declaring the system "easy" |
| Framing effect | Choice changes with wording | "90% survival" vs. "10% mortality" |

## Part 2: Framing Experiment

A disease is expected to kill 600 people. Read both programs and pick one.

- **Program A:** 200 people will be saved.
- **Program B:** 1/3 probability all 600 are saved; 2/3 probability none are saved.

Now the reframe:

- **Program C:** 400 people will die.
- **Program D:** 1/3 probability no one dies; 2/3 probability 600 die.

**Question:** Programs A and C are mathematically identical. So are B and D. Why do most people prefer A over B but D over C? Cite Tversky & Kahneman's **prospect theory** in your answer.
