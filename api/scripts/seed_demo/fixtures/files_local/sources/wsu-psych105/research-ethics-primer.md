# Research Ethics in Behavioral Science — Primer

Every study you read in Psych 105 was reviewed by an ethics board before it ran. The rules behind that review are the result of a hundred years of arguments — and a few scandals that forced the field to write things down. This primer gives you the background to evaluate the ethics of any study you encounter.

## Why We Have Rules: A Short History

- **Tuskegee syphilis study (1932–1972)**: 399 Black men with syphilis were deliberately left untreated for 40 years, even after penicillin became the standard cure in 1947. Public exposure in 1972 prompted the National Research Act (1974).
- **Nuremberg Code (1947)**: first international standard after the Nazi doctors' trial; ten principles, led by *voluntary consent is essential*.
- **Belmont Report (1979)**: the US ethical charter for human-subjects research, issued by the National Commission created by the 1974 Act.

## The Belmont Three

Three principles, in order of priority:

1. **Respect for persons** — individuals are autonomous agents; those with diminished autonomy deserve protection.
2. **Beneficence** — maximize benefits, minimize harms. "Do no harm" is a floor, not a ceiling.
3. **Justice** — fair distribution of research burdens and benefits. No group should disproportionately carry risk while another disproportionately reaps reward.

These translate into three practical requirements: informed consent, risk/benefit assessment, and fair subject selection.

## The IRB (Institutional Review Board)

Any federally funded study involving humans must be reviewed in advance by an IRB. The IRB classifies protocols into three levels:

- **Exempt** — minimal risk, anonymous surveys, public observation.
- **Expedited** — minimal risk, standard methods (brief questionnaires, non-invasive physiology).
- **Full board** — greater than minimal risk, deception, vulnerable populations (children, prisoners, patients).

An IRB can require changes or reject a study outright. Waiving IRB review is not something a researcher gets to decide.

## Informed Consent

Consent must be **informed, voluntary, and documented**. "Informed" means the subject understands:

- The purpose of the study
- What will happen, for how long
- Foreseeable risks and benefits
- That participation is voluntary and can be withdrawn at any time
- How their data will be stored and who will see it

Consent can be waived (e.g., anonymous surveys) but only with IRB approval.

## Deception and the Debrief

Some psychology research (Asch, Milgram, priming studies) requires deception — telling subjects something false so they behave naturally. Deception is permitted only when:

- The scientific value justifies it.
- No reasonable non-deceptive alternative exists.
- The deception is not about risks that would alter consent.
- A **debriefing** follows: the truth is explained, questions answered, distress addressed.

## Special Populations

Children, prisoners, students under the researcher's authority, and clinical populations receive extra protection. Students are especially relevant in Psych 105: "subject pools" must offer an equivalent non-study alternative for course credit so consent is genuinely voluntary.

## Modern Data Ethics

Beyond the Belmont framework:

- **Confidentiality**: separate identifiers from data; encrypt at rest.
- **Data sharing**: default toward openness, but respect subject expectations set at consent.
- **Dual use**: research on manipulation, deepfakes, or surveillance tech has misuse risks beyond individual subjects.
- **Algorithmic research**: studying people via their phones or social-media data without classic consent is an active legal and ethical frontier.

## A Checklist When You Read a Study

- Was IRB approval reported?
- Was consent obtained (or its waiver justified)?
- Were risks to subjects clearly stated?
- If there was deception, was there a debriefing?
- Were vulnerable populations involved, and how were they protected?
- Are the data handled in a way the subjects would recognize as honest?

If any of these is missing, the study's findings may still be interesting — but its authority as evidence is reduced.
