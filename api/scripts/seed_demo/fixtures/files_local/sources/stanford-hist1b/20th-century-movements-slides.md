# 20th-Century Movements: Modernism, Postmodernism, Colonial Critique

## Roadmap

Three interlocking movements. Each reacts against what preceded it. None is neatly over.

## What Is Modernism?

A cross-disciplinary rupture circa 1890–1940. "Make it new" (Ezra Pound, 1934). Shock, abstraction, fragmentation.

## Modernism in Literature

Joyce's *Ulysses* (1922). Eliot's *The Waste Land* (1922). Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* (1925). Kafka's *The Trial* (1925, posth.). Stream of consciousness; mythic structure; collapse of omniscient narration.

## Modernism in Visual Art

Picasso and Braque invent Cubism, 1907–1914. Duchamp's *Fountain* (1917). Mondrian's grids. The Bauhaus (1919–1933).

## Modernism in Music

Stravinsky's *Rite of Spring* (1913) — the Paris riot. Schoenberg's twelve-tone method (1921). Tonality as contingent, not natural.

## Modernist Architecture

Le Corbusier: "A house is a machine for living in" (*Vers une architecture*, 1923). Mies van der Rohe. International Style.

## The Historical Context

World War I (1914–1918) delegitimizes 19th-century narratives of progress. Industrial slaughter demands new forms.

## Freud, Einstein, Saussure

Freud's *The Interpretation of Dreams* (1900) — the unconscious. Einstein's 1905 papers — spacetime. Saussure's *Cours de linguistique générale* (1916) — the arbitrary sign. Reality is mediated.

## The Harlem Renaissance

Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Alain Locke's *The New Negro* (1925). Modernism is not only European.

## Transition: After 1945

Auschwitz. Hiroshima. Adorno: "To write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric" (1949).

## What Is Postmodernism?

Incredulity toward metanarratives (Lyotard, *The Postmodern Condition*, 1979). Pastiche, irony, surface, simulacrum.

## Key Postmodern Thinkers

Derrida, *Of Grammatology* (1967). Foucault, *Discipline and Punish* (1975). Baudrillard, *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981). Jameson, *Postmodernism, or, The Cultural Logic of Late Capitalism* (1991).

## Postmodernism in Practice

Pynchon's *Gravity's Rainbow* (1973). Toni Morrison's *Beloved* (1987). Warhol's Brillo Boxes (1964). Koolhaas's *Delirious New York* (1978).

## Colonial Critique: The Third Wave

Modernism and postmodernism, read from the colony, look different. Who gets to be the subject of "new"?

## Fanon

*Black Skin, White Masks* (1952). *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961). Colonialism as psychic structure.

## Césaire

*Discourse on Colonialism* (1950). European humanism's complicity with fascism: "Hitler is the colonial boomerang."

## Said

*Orientalism* (1978). The Orient as a Western textual production serving imperial power.

## Spivak

"Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988). Representation as double bind.

## Bhabha

*The Location of Culture* (1994). Hybridity, mimicry, ambivalence.

## How the Three Movements Relate

| Movement | Peak | Core Move |
|---|---|---|
| Modernism | 1910–1940 | Break form to register rupture |
| Postmodernism | 1970–1995 | Doubt the break itself |
| Colonial critique | 1950–present | Ask who the "we" has always been |

## Takeaways

Periodization is a political act. The 20th century did not end in 2000. These arguments continue in climate writing, AI ethics, and contemporary fiction.
