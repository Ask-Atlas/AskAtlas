# Enlightenment Thought: Locke, Rousseau, Kant

The European Enlightenment (roughly 1680–1800) reframed political legitimacy, epistemology, and morality around human reason rather than revealed authority. The political wing of the movement is usually read through three thinkers whose social contract arguments still structure modern democratic theory: John Locke, Jean-Jacques Rousseau, and Immanuel Kant.

## John Locke and Natural Rights

Locke's *Two Treatises of Government* (1689) answered Robert Filmer's divine-right defense of monarchy. In the state of nature, Locke argued, individuals possess rights to "life, liberty, and property" grounded in natural law. Government is legitimate only insofar as it protects these rights; when it becomes tyrannical, citizens retain a right of revolution. His *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1690) paired this politics with an empiricist epistemology — the mind as *tabula rasa*, furnished by sensation and reflection. Thomas Jefferson's 1776 *Declaration of Independence* transposes Locke's triad almost verbatim, substituting "pursuit of happiness" for "property."

## Rousseau and the General Will

Rousseau complicates the contract tradition. In the *Discourse on Inequality* (1755) he argues that private property and social comparison corrupt the natural goodness of humanity. His *Social Contract* (1762) opens with the famous line: "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." Legitimacy, for Rousseau, rests not on consent to protect pre-political rights but on citizens collectively authoring law through the *volonté générale* (general will). This reframing prefigures both modern democratic theory and — in critics from Benjamin Constant to Isaiah Berlin — modern totalitarianism.

## Kant: Sapere Aude

Kant's 1784 essay "Beantwortung der Frage: Was ist Aufklärung?" ("An Answer to the Question: What Is Enlightenment?") defines enlightenment as "humanity's emergence from its self-imposed immaturity." His motto: *sapere aude* — dare to know. In the *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785) Kant proposes the categorical imperative: act only on maxims you could will to be universal law. Perpetual Peace (1795) extends this to international relations, imagining a federation of republics as the route to lasting peace.

## Quick Comparison

| Thinker | Work | State of Nature | Legitimacy From |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hobbes | *Leviathan* (1651) | "nasty, brutish, short" | Absolute sovereign |
| Locke | *Two Treatises* (1689) | Peaceful but precarious | Consent + natural rights |
| Rousseau | *Social Contract* (1762) | Innocent, equal | General will |
| Kant | *Perpetual Peace* (1795) | Juridical condition required | Autonomy + universal law |

## Legacy

Enlightenment political thought supplied the vocabulary of modern constitutions (rights, separation of powers, popular sovereignty) and of their critics — from Edmund Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) to 20th-century decolonial readings that ask whose "universal reason" the movement actually served.
