# Globalization in World History

Globalization is older than the word for it. The term itself only entered
academic English in the 1960s and went mainstream after the Cold War, but
the *process* — the integration of distant regions into a shared web of
exchange — has been running for at least two thousand years. HIST 105
treats globalization as a long arc with at least four distinct phases,
each with its own technologies, its own winners, and its own backlash.
Get the phases straight and you can place almost any event on the
{{FILE:wsu-hist105-globalization-timeline}} on the right shelf.

## The four phases

| Phase | Rough dates | Drivers | Limits |
|-------|-------------|---------|--------|
| Archaic | ~200 BCE – 1500 CE | Caravan and monsoon trade, missionary religions | Speed of camel + sail; biological isolation of Americas |
| Proto-globalization | ~1500 – 1800 | Atlantic chartered companies, Columbian Exchange, silver | Wood-and-wind transport; high mortality crossings |
| Industrial globalization | ~1800 – 1914 | Steam, telegraph, gold standard, mass migration | Tariff backlash; collapse with WWI |
| Contemporary globalization | ~1945 – present | Containerization, Bretton Woods, fibre optics, jet travel | Climate cost; political backlash post-2008 |

The dates are conventions, not facts — see
{{GUIDE:hist105-periodization-frameworks}} for why that matters. But the
*shape* of the arc — slow integration, then a sudden acceleration around
1500, then another around 1800 — is real and well-documented.

## Phase 1: the archaic Silk Road and Indian Ocean worlds

The image of "the Silk Road" as a single highway from Xi'an to Antioch
is a romantic fiction invented by the German geographer Ferdinand von
Richthofen in 1877. The reality was a braided network of caravan relay
routes, each leg controlled by different middlemen, with very few
travellers (Marco Polo being the famous exception) covering more than a
fraction of the distance.

Goods that did move across the whole network — Chinese silk, Roman
glass, Central Asian horses, Indian cotton — moved at extraordinary
markup. More important than the trade itself was the **side cargo**:

- Buddhism travelled east into China with Sogdian merchants by the
  second century CE.
- Manichaeism, Nestorian Christianity, and eventually Islam all spread
  along the same routes.
- The bubonic plague of 541 CE (the Plague of Justinian) and the
  fourteenth-century Black Death almost certainly travelled the same
  way.

The Indian Ocean network, driven by predictable monsoon winds, was the
larger and more economically significant arena. Swahili coast city-states
like Kilwa and Sofala connected the African gold trade to Gujarati
textile merchants and Chinese porcelain manufacturers. By 1400, this
was the densest trading zone on earth.

> "The Indian Ocean was, before 1500, the world's busiest highway."
> — K. N. Chaudhuri, *Trade and Civilisation in the Indian Ocean*
> (1985)

## Phase 2: proto-globalization and the Columbian Exchange

The 1492 voyage of Columbus is the conventional pivot, but the more
important date is **1571** — the founding of Manila by Spain, which
closed the loop: silver mined at Potosí (in modern Bolivia) by enslaved
Indigenous and African labour was shipped across the Pacific to Manila,
exchanged for Chinese silk and porcelain, and returned to Acapulco. For
the first time in human history, every inhabited continent except
Australia was inside a single commercial circuit.

The biological side of this integration — the **Columbian Exchange** —
is treated in detail in {{GUIDE:hist105-environment-and-disease}}. For
the economic side, three points are essential:

1. **Silver, not gold, was the engine.** Spanish American silver became
   the de facto global currency, sucked into China's monetized
   tax system after the Single Whip Reform of 1581.
2. **Chartered companies pioneered modern corporate form.** The Dutch
   VOC (1602) and English East India Company (1600) issued tradeable
   shares, paid dividends, and waged private wars.
3. **Slavery was the labour engine.** The Atlantic slave trade moved
   roughly 12.5 million Africans to the Americas between 1500 and 1866.

This was globalization with extraordinary winners and catastrophic
losers, and the asymmetry shaped the next two phases.

## Phase 3: industrial globalization (1800–1914)

The nineteenth century is the period in which integration *measurably*
accelerates. Steam power collapsed the cost of moving bulk goods over
land (railroads) and sea (steamships). The transatlantic telegraph
cable (1866) collapsed the cost of moving information from weeks to
minutes. The gold standard, adopted by most major economies between
1870 and 1900, made cross-border investment routine. Mass migration
moved roughly 50 million Europeans across oceans between 1850 and 1914
— the largest voluntary migration in human history to that point.

The numbers are striking. Between 1800 and 1913, the value of world
trade grew roughly 25-fold. By 1913, the share of world output crossing
borders as exports reached a level that would not be matched again
until the 1970s.

Then it broke. World War I, the interwar tariff wars (Smoot-Hawley,
1930), and the Great Depression rolled back integration so dramatically
that the historian Harold James calls 1914–1945 "the end of
globalization." This collapse is the strongest argument that
globalization is reversible — a useful fact when reading contemporary
debates about deglobalization.

## Phase 4: contemporary globalization (1945–present)

The post-war order rebuilt integration on new institutions: Bretton
Woods (IMF, World Bank, GATT), the United Nations, regional blocs like
the European Coal and Steel Community. The decisive technology was the
**shipping container**, standardized in 1956 by Malcom McLean. By 2020,
about 90% of internationally traded goods spent some part of their
journey in a container. Containerization roughly halved the real cost
of long-distance freight — a much bigger price shock than any tariff
agreement.

Two things distinguish contemporary globalization from its
nineteenth-century predecessor:

- **Capital is far more mobile.** Cross-border financial flows now dwarf
  trade flows in dollar terms.
- **Production itself is fragmented.** The "global value chain" — a
  laptop designed in California, with chips from Taiwan, assembled in
  Vietnam — has no real nineteenth-century analogue.

The 2008 financial crisis, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the 2022 sanctions
on Russia have all stressed this system. Whether we are watching another
1914-style rollback or a re-routing within an integrated framework is
the live debate.

## Common student mistakes

- **Treating globalization as new.** It isn't. The novelty is its
  *speed*, not its existence.
- **Treating it as monocausal.** No single technology, ideology, or
  empire produced any of the four phases.
- **Ignoring the backlash.** Every phase has produced a reaction —
  Mongol-era xenophobia, mercantilism, the 1880s tariff wars, the
  post-2008 populist wave. The backlash is part of the story.
