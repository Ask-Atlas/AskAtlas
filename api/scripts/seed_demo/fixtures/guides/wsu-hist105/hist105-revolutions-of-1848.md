# The Revolutions of 1848

Within roughly twelve months — from January 1848 in Palermo to the
spring of 1849 in Hungary — armed uprisings, mass demonstrations, and
constitutional crises broke out in over fifty European cities. France
deposed a king. The Habsburg empire nearly disintegrated. The German
Confederation tried, and failed, to constitute itself as a nation-
state. The Italian peninsula tried, and failed, to do the same. By
the end of 1849, almost every short-term political demand of the
revolutionaries had been defeated.

And yet by 1880 most of those demands had quietly become reality:
constitutional government, universal male suffrage in much of Western
Europe, the abolition of serfdom in the Habsburg lands, German and
Italian unification. The 1848 revolutions failed in the short term and
won in the long term. Understanding *how* that happened is one of the
clearest illustrations the course offers of the relationship between
political programs and structural change. The lecture deck at
{{FILE:wsu-hist105-revolutions-1848-slides}} maps the geography city
by city; this guide gives you the analytical framework.

## The fuel: three structural pressures

By the late 1840s, three pressures had been building across Europe for
roughly a generation:

1. **Demographic and agrarian crisis.** European population had grown
   from ~140 million in 1750 to ~265 million by 1850. Rural underclasses
   pressed against the limits of the existing land tenure system. The
   1845–1849 potato blight (the Irish famine being the catastrophic
   case, but Belgian, Dutch, and German peasants suffered too) tipped
   chronic food stress into acute crisis.
2. **Industrial dislocation.** The first wave of factory production
   was destroying artisan livelihoods (handloom weavers, journeyman
   printers) faster than industrial employment could absorb them. The
   1840s in particular saw collapsing wages in skilled crafts.
3. **Liberal-nationalist intellectual demand.** A generation of
   educated middle-class men — lawyers, journalists, professors —
   wanted constitutions, parliaments, freedom of the press, and
   nation-states. They were inspired by the unfinished business of
   1789 and by the 1830 Belgian, Polish, and French upheavals.

The crucial point is that the *peasant-and-artisan* grievances and the
*liberal-nationalist* program were never a stable coalition. Each side
needed the other to make a revolution, and as soon as the revolution
took power, the coalition broke apart. That dynamic explains most of
why 1848 failed in the short term.

## The chronology

### February 1848: France

The "February Revolution" in Paris removed King Louis-Philippe and
declared the Second Republic. The provisional government included the
socialist Louis Blanc and committed to "national workshops" — public
employment for the unemployed.

In June, the conservative-liberal majority in the new Constituent
Assembly closed the workshops. Working-class Paris rose in the *June
Days* and was crushed by General Cavaignac with roughly 3,000 dead.
This is the moment liberal France chose property over labour — the
single most consequential decision of the year.

> "In the June Days... the bourgeoisie understood that even the most
> moderate concession to the social republic would shake the
> foundations of bourgeois society."
> — Karl Marx, *The Class Struggles in France* (1850)

### March 1848: the German states and Habsburg empire

News of the February Revolution arrived in Vienna and Berlin within
two weeks. Metternich, the architect of the post-1815 conservative
order, fled Vienna on March 13. Frederick William IV of Prussia
appeared on a balcony in Berlin on March 19 and effectively conceded
the principle of constitutional government.

The Frankfurt Parliament — a self-constituted, elected, all-German
assembly — convened in May 1848 in St. Paul's Church to draft a
national constitution. After eleven months of debate it offered the
imperial crown of a unified Germany to Frederick William IV in March
1849. He refused, calling it a "crown from the gutter." The Frankfurt
project collapsed.

### March–August 1848: the Italian peninsula

Milan and Venice expelled Austrian garrisons. Charles Albert of Sardinia
declared war on Austria in support of Italian unification. Austrian
troops under Radetzky regrouped, won at Custoza (July 1848) and Novara
(March 1849), and restored the status quo. The Roman Republic, briefly
declared in February 1849 with Garibaldi defending the city walls,
fell to a French expeditionary force in July 1849.

### April 1848 – August 1849: Hungary

The Hungarian Diet declared independence from the Habsburg crown in
April 1849. The new emperor, Franz Joseph, called in a Russian army
under Tsar Nicholas I. By August, the Hungarian revolution had been
crushed.

## Why the revolutions failed (short term)

Three interlocking reasons:

- **The liberal-radical coalition broke.** Once peasants got their
  land question addressed (the Habsburg abolition of serfdom in
  September 1848 was the cleanest example), they had no further
  interest in supporting urban radicals.
- **The militaries stayed loyal.** Habsburg, Prussian, and Russian
  armies were primarily composed of peasant conscripts officered by a
  conservative aristocracy. They had no reason to defect.
- **The international conservative order coordinated.** Russia's
  intervention in Hungary, France's intervention in Rome, and Prussia's
  willingness to crush the Frankfurt rump are all examples.

## What 1848 actually changed

Despite the short-term defeats, the long-term effects were enormous:

| Change | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Habsburg abolition of serfdom (1848, ratified 1853) | Conceded by panicked monarchy; never reversed |
| German unification under Prussia (1871) | Bismarck adopted the *national* program of 1848 while excluding the *democratic* program |
| Italian unification (1861, completed 1870) | Same dynamic — Cavour borrows the goal, drops the radicalism |
| Universal male suffrage in France (1848, durable from 1871) | Genuinely new political technology |
| The modern political vocabulary of left-right and "social republic" | Coined in the French debates of February-June 1848 |

This is why historians often call 1848 the "turning point at which
modern history failed to turn" — but quietly added: *and turned anyway,
on a slower timescale*.

## Common student mistakes

- **Calling 1848 a single revolution.** It was at least four — French,
  German, Italian, Hungarian — with different actors, different
  demands, and different outcomes.
- **Treating the failure as total.** It wasn't. The political vocabulary
  and many concrete reforms persisted.
- **Ignoring the colonial echo.** The same 1848 spring saw uprisings
  and slave-emancipation pressure in the French Caribbean. Slavery in
  the French colonies was abolished by decree on April 27, 1848 —
  another genuine, durable 1848 outcome.
