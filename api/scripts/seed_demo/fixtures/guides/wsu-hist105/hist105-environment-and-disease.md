# Environment and Disease in World History

For most of HIST 105's chronological span, the most powerful actors on
the human stage were not states or kings but climate, pathogens, and
soil. The "environmental turn" in world history — gathering pace since
Alfred Crosby's *The Columbian Exchange* (1972) — argues that you
cannot tell the story of empire, conquest, or industrialization without
treating the non-human world as a participant. The
{{FILE:wsu-hist105-env-history-primer}} expands the conceptual toolkit;
this guide gives you the historical narrative.

## The toolkit: four concepts

1. **Carrying capacity** — the maximum population a region can support
   given its current technology. Pre-industrial Europe ran near its
   carrying capacity for centuries; collapses (1315–1322 famine, the
   Black Death) released the Malthusian pressure temporarily.
2. **Ecological imperialism** — Crosby's term for the way European
   crops, livestock, and pathogens established themselves in temperate
   zones outside Europe (the Americas, Australia, New Zealand) more
   thoroughly than European *people* did.
3. **Virgin-soil epidemics** — outbreaks in populations with no prior
   immunity. The post-1492 Americas are the textbook case; the Pacific
   islands in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries are a parallel
   case study.
4. **The Anthropocene** — the proposed geological epoch defined by
   measurable human transformation of the planet. The candidate start
   dates (1610, 1784, 1945) each tell a different story about which
   humans, doing what.

## The Black Death (1346–1353)

The bubonic plague pandemic that reached Europe in 1347 killed roughly
30–50% of the European population in five years and similar fractions
of populations in North Africa and the Middle East. The disease likely
originated in Central Asia and rode the Mongol-era trade networks
discussed in {{GUIDE:hist105-globalization-overview}} into the
Mediterranean.

The political and economic effects are textbook material:

- **Wages rose** as labour became scarce, which broke the rigidities of
  manorial serfdom in Western Europe.
- **The Catholic Church's authority cracked** — it could neither
  explain nor prevent the catastrophe, and clerical mortality was
  extreme.
- **Persecution intensified** — Jewish communities in particular were
  scapegoated and massacred.

The Black Death is the single best illustration of the principle that
diseases do not respect political borders, and that biological
catastrophe can permanently rearrange social structures.

## The Columbian Exchange (1492 onward)

When Columbus reached the Caribbean in October 1492, he ended roughly
12,000 years of biological isolation between the Americas and the rest
of the world. The two-way exchange that followed reshaped diet,
agriculture, and demography on every continent.

### Eastward (Americas → Old World)

| Crop | Effect |
|------|--------|
| Maize | Calorie-dense staple; major Chinese and African upland crop by 1700 |
| Potato | Doubled or tripled per-acre calorie yields in northern Europe |
| Tomato, chili, cacao | Reshaped Mediterranean, South Asian, and West African cuisines |
| Sweet potato | Famine-resistant crop in southern China; population enabler |

The standard estimate is that crops of American origin enabled roughly
a quarter of the global population growth between 1700 and 1900. The
*demographic* gift of the Americas to the Old World was almost as large
as the *demographic* cost of the Old World to the Americas.

### Westward (Old World → Americas)

The headline cargo was disease. Smallpox arrived in Hispaniola by 1518
and reached the Aztec empire in 1520. Measles, influenza, typhus,
mumps, and later yellow fever followed. Estimates of the total
demographic collapse vary widely — from a "low" 50% to a "high" 90% of
the pre-contact American population dead within 150 years. Even at the
low end, this is the worst demographic catastrophe in recorded human
history.

> "It is incredible how the people are wasted by the smallpox.... They
> die in heaps, like cattle stricken with the murrain."
> — Bartolomé de las Casas, *Brevísima relación de la destrucción de
> las Indias* (1542)

The political consequence is that the Spanish *did not have to conquer*
much of the Americas in the conventional military sense. They walked
into hollowed-out polities. The Cortés-vs-Moctezuma narrative seriously
underweights the role of smallpox in the 1520 Aztec collapse.

The Old World also gained Old World livestock — horses, pigs, cattle,
sheep, chickens — which restructured Indigenous economies on the
Plains, the Argentine pampas, and the South African veld. The
"horse-Indian" cultures of the Lakota and Comanche are a *post-Columbian*
phenomenon, dating to the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

## The Little Ice Age (~1300–1850)

A multi-century cooling of roughly 0.5–1.0°C below twentieth-century
averages produced concentrated effects in the seventeenth century: bad
harvests, glacial advances, and political crises in regions as distant
as Ming China (which fell in 1644) and the British Isles (English Civil
War, 1642–1651). Geoffrey Parker's *Global Crisis* (2013) argues that
the Little Ice Age is the missing variable that connects these
otherwise unrelated upheavals.

The Little Ice Age is also a useful corrective to monocausal climate
narratives: it did not, by itself, cause anything. It tightened the
constraints inside which political and economic systems already under
stress could fail.

## The Great Acceleration (1945–)

After 1945, almost every measurable human impact on the planet —
atmospheric CO₂, fertilizer use, freshwater withdrawal, plastic
production, large-dam construction, paper consumption — turns sharply
upward and stays there. The "Great Acceleration" graphs (Steffen et
al., 2015) are the most-cited single figure in Anthropocene scholarship.

Three things to take from this:

- The ecological footprint of the post-war boom is qualitatively
  different from any earlier period. The graphs do not bend; they
  hockey-stick.
- The acceleration is *unequally distributed*. Per-capita carbon
  emissions in the US are roughly 14× those of sub-Saharan Africa.
- Climate change makes the older HIST 105 question — *how do societies
  respond when the environment moves underneath them?* — newly urgent.

## How to write about environment and disease

A useful HIST 105 essay structure:

1. Identify a non-human actor (climate shift, pathogen, ecological
   transfer).
2. Specify a polity or region and a date range.
3. Show how the non-human actor changed what humans could plausibly
   do.
4. **Avoid the determinist trap.** Environment shapes the menu of
   choices, not the choices themselves.
