# Periodization in World History

Periodization is the act of dividing the past into named chunks — "the
Middle Ages," "the Early Modern period," "the Anthropocene." It feels like
a neutral act of housekeeping. It is not. Every periodization is an
argument about what changed, when it changed, and for whom it mattered.
HIST 105 returns to this question constantly, and the WSU Roots of
Contemporary Issues (RCI) framework is itself a deliberately chosen
periodization. Understanding why instructors picked the breakpoints they
did — roughly 1500, 1800, 1914, 1989 — is half the work of the course.

## Why periodization matters

Consider the label *Middle Ages*. The phrase implies a "middle" between
two more important periods (classical antiquity and the Renaissance). It
was coined by Italian humanists in the fifteenth century who wanted to
position themselves as the heirs of Rome and dismiss the thousand years
in between as a regrettable detour. That polemical move still shapes
introductory textbooks today, even though most professional medievalists
have spent the last fifty years arguing that the period was neither dark
nor a mere bridge.

The lesson generalizes:

- A periodization always **privileges some change over others**.
- A periodization is always **made from a particular vantage point**.
- A periodization always **carries values forward** from the moment it
  was coined.

Whenever you encounter a date on a timeline, ask: who decided this date
mattered, and what are they implicitly telling you to ignore?

## The WSU RCI breakpoints

The HIST 105 course narrative leans on five rough breakpoints. Each one
is defended somewhere in the lecture deck linked at
{{FILE:wsu-hist105-periodization-guide}}; you should be able to give a
two-sentence justification for each on the midterm.

| Year   | Why it matters | Counter-argument |
|--------|---------------|------------------|
| 1450   | Printing press, Ottoman conquest of Constantinople, Iberian Atlantic voyages begin | Centred on Europe; Ming China and Mughal expansion don't pivot here |
| 1500   | Columbian Exchange begins to globalize biota | A round-number convenience; the exchange ramped up over decades |
| 1800   | Industrial Revolution measurably accelerates energy use per capita | Industrial change is patchy outside Britain until ~1850 |
| 1914   | First truly global industrial war | World War I is part of a broader 1880–1945 crisis arc |
| 1989   | End of the bipolar Cold War order | Many scholars now see the global financial crisis (2008) as a stronger break |

Notice that two of the five dates explicitly recognize their European
bias. That is honest periodization. Dishonest periodization hides the
choice.

## Three competing frameworks

### 1. The "long centuries" model

Borrowed from European historiography, the long-century model groups
events around political/cultural turning points: the *long sixteenth
century* (~1450–1640) covers the Reformation and the early Atlantic
world; the *long nineteenth century* (~1789–1914) covers the dual
revolutions through to World War I. It is useful when politics and ideas
are the foreground. It is unhelpful when ecology, demography, or trade
move on different rhythms.

### 2. World-systems and longue durée

Fernand Braudel and his successors argued that the meaningful unit of
analysis is the *world-system*: a network of unequal exchange spanning
several political units. The pivots that matter are not the death of a
king but the integration of a new region into the system. Under this
model, 1500 is critical because the Americas joined the Eurasian
exchange. So is 1800 — coal-powered Britain pulls ahead of comparably
developed regions in China and India.

> "Events are dust... only long-time scales reveal the underlying
> currents of history."
> — Fernand Braudel, *On History* (1969)

### 3. Anthropocene-aware periodization

A more recent move treats the human transformation of the planet as the
true backbone of modern history. The candidate breakpoints are:

- **1610** — the lowest atmospheric CO₂ point following the demographic
  collapse of the Americas (the "Orbis Spike" hypothesis)
- **1784** — Watt's improved steam engine
- **1945** — the Trinity test and the start of the Great Acceleration

Each carries its own politics. The Orbis Spike, in particular, makes the
Columbian Exchange not just an economic event but the first geological
trace of empire.

## How to use periodization in essays

When you write about a long period, declare your periodization in the
first paragraph and defend it. Three sentences is enough:

> "This essay treats 1450–1800 as a single 'early modern' period
> because the Columbian Exchange, the printing press, and the rise of
> chartered companies all reorganized the same web of exchange. I am
> aware that this framing privileges Atlantic developments over the
> Indian Ocean world; where that distorts the argument, I will note it."

That two-sentence move — *here is my framework, and here is its blind
spot* — is the single most efficient way to demonstrate historiographical
maturity to a HIST 105 grader.

## Common student mistakes

- **Treating dates as facts.** "The Renaissance began in 1400" is a
  convention, not an event you could have witnessed.
- **Mixing scales.** Don't compare a political event (e.g. 1789) to a
  demographic process (e.g. the post-1500 American population collapse)
  as if they have the same kind of edges.
- **Assuming Eurocentric dates apply globally.** "The eighteenth century"
  in Qing China is not the same animal as "the eighteenth century" in
  France, even though the calendar agrees.
