# Primary Source Analysis

A primary source is any artefact produced *during* the period being
studied — a letter, a diary, a tax record, a sermon, a photograph, a
piece of legislation, a satirical novel, a piece of pottery. A
*secondary source* is what historians later wrote about those primary
sources. The most common HIST 105 exam question is: "Read the
following short excerpt. Identify it. Place it in context. Tell me
what it's good for and what it's not." The {{FILE:wsu-hist105-primary-source-worksheet}}
gives you a tabular form to fill in for any source you encounter; this
guide explains the moves the worksheet asks you to make.

## The five-question framework (memorize this)

For any primary source, answer five questions in order:

1. **What is it?** Letter, official decree, novel, photograph, tax
   roll, oral history transcript? The genre determines what kinds of
   claims it can support.
2. **Who made it, when, and for whom?** A papal bull from 1493 by
   Alexander VI is not the same evidence as a peasant's letter from
   1493 to her son.
3. **What does it say?** Summarize the literal content in two
   sentences.
4. **What does it assume but not say?** This is where almost all of
   the analytical work lives. What did the author take for granted?
   What did the intended audience already know?
5. **What is it useful for, and what is it bad evidence for?** Every
   source is good evidence of *something*; equally importantly, every
   source is bad evidence of *something else*.

## Worked example #1: Burckhardt on the Renaissance

Open {{FILE:wikimedia-burckhardt-renaissance-italy-pdf}} (or, for
EPUB, {{FILE:gutenberg-burckhardt-renaissance-epub}}) to Part II,
"The Development of the Individual."

> "In the Middle Ages... man was conscious of himself only as a
> member of a race, people, party, family, or corporation — only
> through some general category. In Italy this veil first melted
> into air."
> — Jacob Burckhardt (1860)

Apply the five questions:

1. **What is it?** A passage from a long historical-cultural essay
   published in book form in 1860 in Basel.
2. **Who made it, when, for whom?** A Swiss professor of history,
   educated middle-class German-reading audience, written in the wake
   of the failed 1848 revolutions and the consolidation of new
   national states.
3. **What does it say?** That medieval Europeans only experienced
   themselves through collective identities, and that Renaissance
   Italy was the first place this changed.
4. **What does it assume?** That "the individual" is a coherent
   trans-historical concept. That medieval Europeans had no inner life
   (a strong claim modern medievalists reject). That the relevant unit
   of comparison is "Italy" rather than, say, particular Florentine
   guilds.
5. **What is it useful for?** Understanding *nineteenth-century*
   intellectual debates about the origins of European modernity. It
   is, by itself, very weak evidence about *fifteenth-century*
   Florence. It is excellent evidence about how 1860 Basel saw the
   fifteenth century.

This last move — *Burckhardt is a primary source for nineteenth-century
historiography even though he is a secondary source for the
Renaissance* — is the kind of analytical pivot that distinguishes B
work from A work in HIST 105.

## Worked example #2: a literary source

A literary source can be a primary source for several different things
at once. Take {{FILE:gutenberg-huckleberry-finn-txt}} — Mark Twain's
*The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn*, published 1884, set in roughly
the 1840s on the Mississippi River.

What is it good evidence of?

- 1880s American attitudes toward slavery, race, and childhood (it is
  a primary source about the *post-Reconstruction* moment of its
  publication).
- The literary technique of vernacular first-person narration as a
  vehicle for moral satire.
- Twain's specific moral and political project.

What is it *bad* evidence of?

- The lived experience of enslaved people in the 1840s. The character
  Jim is filtered through a white child narrator and a white adult
  author and a national-mythology-building publication moment.
- The actual sociology of slavery on the Mississippi. The novel
  compresses, romanticizes, and ironizes its setting.

The general lesson: *literature is rich evidence about the moment of
its production and weak evidence about the period it depicts*. That
inversion catches a lot of HIST 105 students.

## Worked example #3: a quantitative source

Imagine you are handed a parish baptismal register from a Yorkshire
village covering 1700–1750: just dates, names, and parents' names. A
HIST 105 prompt might ask you what this source can tell you.

What it can tell you:

- Crude birth rates and seasonality
- Frequency of common names (a proxy for cultural change)
- Survival of children to baptismal age
- Family naming patterns (a proxy for inheritance customs)

What it cannot tell you:

- Anything about births that were never baptized (illegitimate, infant
  mortality before baptism, religious dissenters)
- Causes of death
- Anything about the experiences of the people listed

Quantitative sources are *very* good evidence of what they
systematically record and *systematically silent* about everything
else. The silences matter.

## Common student mistakes

- **Treating a source as a window onto the past.** It is a window
  onto its *author*. The past is on the other side of the author, not
  the source itself.
- **Confusing rhetoric with description.** A polemical pamphlet that
  describes a king as a tyrant is good evidence that *some people
  thought he was a tyrant*. It is not, by itself, evidence that he
  was one.
- **Citing secondary sources as if they were primary.** Wikipedia is
  not a primary source about the French Revolution.
- **Forgetting the audience.** A speech to parliament is meant to
  persuade parliament. A diary entry is (sometimes) meant to persuade
  the future. A tax form is meant to satisfy an auditor. The intended
  audience shapes everything.

## Citation hygiene

For HIST 105 essays, cite primary sources by the author, short title,
date, and the location in the source (page, chapter, line, archival
folder):

> Burckhardt, *Civilization of the Renaissance in Italy* (1860), Part
> II, ch. 1.

Do not cite "the textbook" as a primary source. Don't cite a URL alone
unless the source is born-digital — give the print original where
possible.
