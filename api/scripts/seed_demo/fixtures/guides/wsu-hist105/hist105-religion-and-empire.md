# Religion and Empire: Renaissance, Reformation, and Imperial Order

The textbook narrative treats the Renaissance and the Reformation as
two separate "movements" — one cultural, one religious — that happen
to overlap in the sixteenth century. HIST 105 takes a more useful
view: both are *symptoms* of an underlying transformation in how
authority, knowledge, and imperial power were organized in Europe and,
through European empires, much of the rest of the world. To work
through this guide, you will want the classic monographs from the
attached files — Burckhardt's *Civilization of the Renaissance in
Italy* ({{FILE:wikimedia-burckhardt-renaissance-italy-pdf}}, also at
{{FILE:gutenberg-burckhardt-renaissance-epub}}), the
{{FILE:wikimedia-reformation-italy-pdf}}, and the popular synthesis at
{{FILE:gutenberg-beacon-lights-renaissance-reformation-epub}} — open in
another tab.

## Part 1: the Italian Renaissance reconsidered

Jacob Burckhardt's *Die Cultur der Renaissance in Italien* (1860) is
the foundational text for the modern conception of the Renaissance. It
argued that Italian city-states between roughly 1300 and 1500 produced
the first genuinely *modern* European culture: secular politics,
individual self-fashioning, a new relationship to classical antiquity,
and a marketplace of artistic patronage. Burckhardt's framing has
shaped textbooks for over 150 years.

But three caveats are required if you cite Burckhardt in an essay:

1. **He overstated the secularism.** Renaissance Florence was an
   intensely religious city. The patrons who commissioned Botticelli
   and Michelangelo were also paying for masses for their souls.
2. **He underplayed continuity with the Middle Ages.** Most of what
   Burckhardt called "rediscovery" of classical texts had been quietly
   happening since the twelfth century.
3. **He centred Italy.** Renaissance-style humanism had Northern
   European, Iberian, Ottoman, and Persian variants that he ignored.

> "In the Middle Ages... man was conscious of himself only as a member
> of a race, people, party, family, or corporation — only through some
> general category. In Italy this veil first melted into air... man
> became a spiritual *individual*, and recognised himself as such."
> — Jacob Burckhardt, *The Civilization of the Renaissance in Italy*
> (1860), Part II

What HIST 105 wants you to take from the Renaissance:

- A new vocabulary of *human dignity* and *civic virtue* that will
  feed directly into the Reformation, the Scientific Revolution, and
  eventually Enlightenment political theory.
- The printing press (Gutenberg, ~1450) that made all of the above
  scalable.
- A patronage system in which wealthy merchants and bishops competed
  for prestige through cultural production — a pattern recognizable in
  modern philanthropy.

## Part 2: the Reformation as media event

Luther's 95 Theses, posted (or, more likely, mailed) on October 31,
1517, were not the first protest against Catholic institutional
practice. Wycliffe (1380s), Hus (1410s), and the Lollard tradition had
been raising similar concerns for over a century, with little
political traction. What changed in 1517 was the printing press.

Within two months, Luther's Theses were reprinted in Leipzig,
Nuremberg, and Basel. Within a year, German-language pamphlets
explaining his position were circulating in tens of thousands of
copies. By 1525, Luther was arguably the best-selling author in Europe.
The *Reformation* was the first European event to play out at the
speed of print.

This matters for two HIST 105 themes:

- **Technology + ideology = scale.** The same compound is how you read
  the Enlightenment, the French Revolution, and the late-twentieth-
  century internet revolutions. The Reformation is the prototype.
- **The fragmentation of Christendom that followed reshaped imperial
  politics for two centuries.** The Treaty of Augsburg (1555), the
  Edict of Nantes (1598), the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648), and the
  Treaty of Westphalia (1648) are all aftershocks.

The Westphalian settlement is often credited as the birth of the
modern state system. The principle that each sovereign decides the
religion of his own territory (*cuius regio, eius religio*) actually
originated at the **Peace of Augsburg (1555)** and was reaffirmed and
extended (to include Calvinism) at Westphalia (1648). That is a strong
historiographical claim worth interrogating, but it is the framing
most HIST 105 graders expect you to engage with.

## Part 3: empire as religious project

Iberian Catholic expansion into the Americas was framed by the Spanish
and Portuguese crowns as a religious mission. The 1493 papal bull
*Inter Caetera* divided the non-Christian world between the two
empires; the Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) drew the line. From the
crown's perspective, conquest, conversion, and silver extraction were
the same project.

This had concrete consequences:

- The *encomienda* system bound Indigenous labour to Spanish
  *encomenderos* in exchange for nominal religious instruction.
- The Inquisition operated in the Americas (Mexico City and Lima
  tribunals from 1571) as well as in Iberia.
- Bartolomé de las Casas's 1550–1551 debate at Valladolid with Juan
  Ginés de Sepúlveda was the first sustained European argument over
  whether Indigenous peoples possessed full humanity. The judges
  issued no formal verdict — historians still contest who "won" —
  but Sepúlveda's defense of conquest by force was suppressed from
  publication, and the *encomienda* system continued largely unchanged
  on the ground.

Ottoman, Mughal, and Safavid empires all also wove religion into
imperial legitimacy in the same period — the Ottoman caliphate claim
after 1517, the Mughal *millet*-style accommodation of religious
pluralism under Akbar (r. 1556–1605), the Safavid imposition of
Twelver Shi'ism. The point is not that European empires were unique;
the point is that *imperial-scale religion* is a sixteenth- and
seventeenth-century Eurasian phenomenon.

## Part 4: the Wars of Religion and the science backlash

Roughly a century of intermittent European warfare over religious
settlement — the French Wars of Religion (1562–1598), the Eighty Years'
War (1568–1648), the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648) — produced a
generation of intellectuals who concluded that *theological certainty
was politically dangerous*. This is the soil from which the Scientific
Revolution and the Enlightenment grow.

The Royal Society of London's motto, adopted in 1660, was *Nullius in
verba* — "take no one's word for it." That is, in part, a Reformation-
era response to a century of confessional warfare: build a community
of knowledge that does not depend on any cleric's authority.

## Common student mistakes

- **Treating the Renaissance as anti-religious.** It wasn't. It was
  religious differently.
- **Assuming the Reformation was a simple Catholic-vs-Protestant
  split.** Within Protestantism, Lutheran, Calvinist, Anabaptist, and
  Zwinglian streams disagreed sharply. Within Catholicism, the
  Counter-Reformation (especially the Jesuits, founded 1540) was a
  serious internal renewal, not just a backlash.
- **Forgetting the imperial frame.** Religion in 1500–1700 is empire
  business. Don't write about Luther without writing about Charles V.
