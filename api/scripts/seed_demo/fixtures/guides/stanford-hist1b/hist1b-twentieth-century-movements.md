# Twentieth-Century Movements

The half-century after 1945 is the densest, most argued-over stretch of HIST 1B, partly because much of it is still inside living memory and partly because so many of its conflicts remain unresolved. This unit treats the period as five overlapping movements — Cold War, decolonization, civil rights, second-wave feminism, and the long 1989 — and follows each from origin through high point through (provisional) settlement.

## The Cold War (1945–1991)

The Cold War was simultaneously a strategic rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union, an ideological contest between two universalist political programs, and a civil war inside many nominally non-aligned states. Conventional periodization runs:

- **First Cold War (1945–62).** Berlin Blockade (1948–49); NATO (1949); Korean War (1950–53); Khrushchev's "secret speech" denouncing Stalin (1956); Hungarian uprising (1956); Sputnik (1957); Berlin Wall (1961); Cuban Missile Crisis (October 1962). The closeness of catastrophe in 1962 — McNamara later judged the world had been "one phone call away" from nuclear war — produced the first arms-control architecture (Partial Test Ban Treaty 1963; Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty 1968).
- **Détente (1969–79).** SALT I (1972); the Helsinki Accords (1975), which the Soviets signed for the territorial guarantees and the West used the human-rights provisions of to fund dissident networks; the Vietnamese reunification (1975); the Sino-American opening (Nixon's 1972 visit).
- **Second Cold War (1979–85).** Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (December 1979); NATO Pershing II deployment (1983); Reagan's Strategic Defense Initiative (1983); Korean Air Lines 007 shootdown (1983).
- **Endgame (1985–91).** Gorbachev (general secretary March 1985); *glasnost* and *perestroika*; the revolutions of 1989; German reunification (October 1990); the dissolution of the Soviet Union (December 1991).

For HIST 1B, two analytical points matter. First, the Cold War was not "cold" in the global South — it was fought hot in Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Mozambique, Nicaragua, Afghanistan, and dozens of other places, with combined death tolls in the millions. Second, the Cold War shaped Western domestic politics in ways that outlasted it: the Marshall Plan (1948) and the European Coal and Steel Community (1951) — the institutional ancestors of today's European Union — were both Cold-War-era projects.

## Decolonization (1945–1975)

Between 1945 and 1975 the great European maritime empires dissolved. India and Pakistan partitioned in August 1947 with roughly a million dead. Indonesian independence was recognized in 1949 after four years of war. Ghana became the first sub-Saharan African colony to gain independence (1957); fifteen more African states followed in the "Year of Africa" (1960). The French war in Algeria (1954–62) ended in independence at a cost of perhaps half a million Algerian dead. Portugal's empire collapsed last, after the Carnation Revolution in Lisbon (April 1974) ended thirteen years of African counter-insurgency.

Decolonization mattered for HIST 1B in three ways:

1. **It ended European hegemony in fact, not just in rhetoric.** By 1975 the European powers that had effectively governed half the planet in 1900 governed almost none of it.
2. **It reshaped Western politics.** Decolonization war (especially Algeria for France, Indochina and Algeria for the United States, Suez 1956 for Britain) produced political crises that toppled governments and reshaped political identities at home.
3. **It produced "post-colonial" intellectual programs** — Frantz Fanon's *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961), Edward Said's *Orientalism* (1978) — that have, in turn, transformed Western humanities curricula.

## Civil rights, feminism, and 1968

The 1960s "movement" cluster is best disaggregated. In the United States the **civil rights movement** moved from the Montgomery bus boycott (1955–56) and *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) through the Civil Rights Act (1964) and the Voting Rights Act (1965) to the assassinations of Malcolm X (1965) and Martin Luther King Jr. (1968). In Europe the May 1968 events — student occupation of Nanterre and the Sorbonne, the largest general strike in French history — briefly destabilized the Fifth Republic. The Prague Spring (January–August 1968) was crushed by Warsaw Pact intervention; the Mexico City Olympics (October 1968) opened with the Tlatelolco massacre.

**Second-wave feminism** ran on a slightly different clock. Betty Friedan's *The Feminine Mystique* (1963) named the postwar suburban predicament; *Roe v. Wade* (1973) constitutionalized a federal abortion right in the United States; the U.K. Sex Discrimination Act passed in 1975. In France, Simone de Beauvoir's *The Second Sex* (1949) had supplied the philosophical groundwork two decades earlier. In Italy and Spain, divorce was legalized in 1970 and 1981 respectively. The intellectual reorientation — that gender, like race, is a social and political category and not a natural fact — has shaped academic and popular discourse across the rest of the period.

## The "long 1989"

The collapse of communist regimes in Eastern Europe in 1989 looked, at the time, almost too symmetrical to be true: Poland (Solidarity-led elections, June), Hungary (border opening, August), East Germany (Wall, 9 November), Czechoslovakia (Velvet Revolution, November–December), Romania (Ceaușescu executed, December). Within two years the Soviet Union itself dissolved (December 1991).

Three retrospective points are worth holding:

- **It was not foreseen.** Neither Western Sovietologists nor the CIA predicted the speed of collapse. The lesson — that political legitimacy is more brittle than it looks — is one historians of long-run regimes (the Reformation, 1789, 1848) recognize on sight.
- **The triumphalism was overstated.** Francis Fukuyama's "end of history" thesis (1989, expanded 1992) was the high-water mark of post-Cold-War liberal confidence. The two decades since have not been kind to it.
- **The settlement was fragile.** NATO and EU expansion (1999, 2004, 2007), the Yugoslav wars (1991–2001), the rise of post-Soviet authoritarianism, the 2008 financial crisis, and the politics of the 2010s have all reopened questions the 1990s thought closed.

## How to study this unit

The temptation in a unit this dense is to memorize headlines. Resist it. Two methodological habits will serve you better:

1. **Pair each event with one primary source** — a speech, a treaty clause, a photograph, a constitution. Build the dossier yourself; don't rely on the textbook's selection. The {{GUIDE:hist1b-close-reading-method}} unit covers the technique.
2. **Track institutions, not personalities.** The Marshall Plan, the EEC, the U.N. General Assembly, the Voting Rights Act, *Roe v. Wade*, NATO Article 5 — these are the durable objects. Personalities (Kennedy, de Gaulle, Thatcher, Gorbachev) matter, but institutional change is what survives them.

Refer to {{FILE:stanford-hist1b-key-dates-timeline}} for the chronological scaffold, and use it actively — annotate, contest, extend.

## Closing the course

The half-century from Yalta to Maastricht is the period in which the categories you have inherited — democracy, market economy, human rights, national self-determination — became globally portable, and also the period in which their tensions became internal to most states rather than between them. The historiographical literacy this course aims at — distinguishing what changed from what merely renamed itself, and being precise about both — is the skill the next courses in your major will assume you have.
