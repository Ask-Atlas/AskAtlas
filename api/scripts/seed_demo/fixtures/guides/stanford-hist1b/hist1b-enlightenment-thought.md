# Enlightenment Thought

The Enlightenment is a notoriously slippery object. It is sometimes a single coherent movement, sometimes a constellation of national variants (French *philosophes*, Scottish moral philosophers, German *Aufklärer*, Neapolitan reformers), sometimes a longer eighteenth-century mood. For HIST 1B we treat it as a set of overlapping intellectual programs, roughly 1680–1800, sharing three commitments:

1. **The competent use of reason** — by individuals, in public — is the proper test of received authority.
2. **Religious authority is not exempt** from that test, though most Enlighteners were neither atheists nor revolutionaries.
3. **A reading public**, served by newspapers, periodicals, coffeehouses, salons, and circulating libraries, is the medium through which reason does its work.

## Pre-history: the late seventeenth century

Three near-simultaneous developments in the 1680s set the agenda. First, the scientific revolution culminated in Newton's *Principia Mathematica* (1687), which offered a single mathematical description of terrestrial and celestial motion and became, for the next century, the model of what reasoned inquiry could achieve. Second, John Locke (1632–1704) published the *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) — empiricist epistemology — and *Two Treatises of Government* (1689) — natural rights, government by consent, the right of resistance. Third, Pierre Bayle (1647–1706), a Huguenot exile in Rotterdam, produced his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697), an erudite skeptical encyclopedia whose footnotes systematically destabilized claims that pious or scholastic readers had taken for granted.

These three projects are usually pre-Enlightenment in textbook chronology, but the eighteenth century read them as foundational. When Voltaire writes that "if God did not exist, it would be necessary to invent him" (*Épître à l'auteur du livre des Trois Imposteurs*, 1768), he is doing late Bayle.

## The French *philosophes*

The most visible Enlightenment was French. Its central monument is the *Encyclopédie, ou Dictionnaire raisonné des sciences, des arts et des métiers*, edited by Denis Diderot and Jean d'Alembert and published in 28 folio volumes between 1751 and 1772. Roughly 4,000 buyers — many of them outside France — subscribed to a work whose entries combined practical technology (engravings of looms, forges, and printing presses) with carefully placed cross-references that nudged readers toward conclusions the censors would not have approved had they been stated openly. The *Encyclopédie* is, among other things, a textbook example of how to write subversive prose under a censorship regime.

Around the *Encyclopédie* clustered the writers we still read:

- **Voltaire** (1694–1778). *Letters Concerning the English Nation* (1733), *Candide* (1759), the campaigns to rehabilitate Calas and Sirven. Voltaire's contribution was less a system than a tone — wit weaponized against fanaticism — and an institutional template for the public intellectual.
- **Montesquieu** (1689–1755). *The Spirit of the Laws* (1748) compared political constitutions and proposed the separation of powers. *Persian Letters* (1721) used a fictional ethnographic frame to defamiliarize French institutions.
- **Rousseau** (1712–1778). The cuckoo in the *philosophe* nest — sentimental rather than ironic, suspicious of commercial society, convinced that the arts and sciences had corrupted morals (*Discourse on the Sciences and Arts*, 1750). *The Social Contract* (1762) reframed political legitimacy in terms of the general will.

> "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." — Rousseau, *Du contrat social*, opening line.

## Scottish, German, and other Enlightenments

To stop at France is to mistake one variant for the whole. The **Scottish Enlightenment** — David Hume (1711–1776), Adam Smith (1723–1790), Adam Ferguson, Hutcheson — produced empiricist epistemology, sentimentalist moral philosophy, and the first systematic political economy (Smith's *Wealth of Nations*, 1776). The **German Aufklärung** culminated in Immanuel Kant (1724–1804), whose three Critiques (1781, 1788, 1790) re-grounded epistemology, ethics, and aesthetics on the conditions of possible human experience. Kant's short essay "Was ist Aufklärung?" (1784) remains the cleanest one-page definition of the movement:

> "Enlightenment is the human being's emergence from his self-incurred minority. Minority is inability to make use of one's own understanding without direction from another… *Sapere aude!* Have courage to make use of your own understanding! is thus the motto of enlightenment."

In Naples, Giambattista Vico's *Scienza Nuova* (1725, third edition 1744) sketched a historicist alternative to Cartesian rationalism. In Königsberg, Moses Mendelssohn argued for Jewish civic emancipation. In British North America, *philosophe* vocabulary was repurposed for the political projects covered in {{GUIDE:hist1b-age-of-revolutions}}.

## Religion: not what you may have been told

A common student error is to treat the Enlightenment as wholesale anti-religious. With a handful of exceptions (Holbach, Diderot in private), it was not. Most Enlighteners worked some version of one of three positions:

- **Deism** — God designed the universe but does not intervene; revealed religion is at best superfluous.
- **Latitudinarianism** — minimize doctrinal dispute; tolerate sects within a broad Protestant or Catholic establishment.
- **Reformed orthodoxy in Enlightenment dress** — defend Christianity, but on rational rather than authoritarian grounds (Joseph Butler, William Paley).

What the eighteenth century did do — and this is the durable change — was move religion out of the place where it could not be discussed without violence. The religious wars of the previous century (see {{GUIDE:hist1b-reformation}}) had taught a hard lesson; toleration, even when grudging, was the practical settlement.

## The public sphere

Jürgen Habermas's *Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* (1962) named the social infrastructure that made all of this possible: a bourgeois public sphere of coffeehouses, salons, periodicals (the *Tatler* and *Spectator* in London from 1709), reading societies, and Masonic lodges in which strangers could discuss ideas as equals on rational grounds. The Habermasian model has been heavily revised — historians have shown that women, plebeian readers, and colonial subjects participated in ways the original account underplayed — but the basic claim survives: the eighteenth century built the institutional shell within which modern politics still operates.

## Bridging to the next unit

By 1789 the Enlightenment had supplied a vocabulary — rights, consent, public opinion, separation of powers, religious toleration — that revolutionary actors in America, France, and Saint-Domingue would deploy in earnest. It had not, however, supplied a politics. Enlighteners disagreed sharply about whether monarchy could be reformed, whether commerce was civilizing, whether the people were ready to govern themselves. The next unit, {{GUIDE:hist1b-age-of-revolutions}}, follows what happens when Enlightenment categories meet political pressure that does not wait for philosophers to agree.
