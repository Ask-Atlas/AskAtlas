# Close Reading: A Method for Primary Sources

Close reading is the single most transferable skill HIST 1B teaches. Almost every assessment in this course — the source-analysis paper, the document-based exam question, the seminar contribution — depends on it. The good news is that it is a skill, not a talent: a deliberate routine that anyone can learn and that improves visibly with practice. This guide describes the routine, walks through two worked examples, and lists the questions that distinguish description from analysis.

For the printable worksheet that codifies the steps below, use {{FILE:stanford-hist1b-close-reading-worksheet}}.

## What close reading is — and isn't

Close reading is *not* paraphrasing. A paraphrase says what the source says in slightly different words; close reading says what the source is *doing* and how it does it. Close reading is also not "reading between the lines" in the sense of inventing hidden meanings. Every claim a close reading makes should be anchored in the words on the page.

A useful working definition: close reading treats a primary source as a constructed artifact — a thing that was made, by a particular author, for a particular audience, in a particular medium, at a particular moment — and asks how the construction shapes what the source can mean.

## The four-step routine

Use this routine on every primary source you read for this course. With practice it takes ten to fifteen minutes per page; the discipline is in not skipping any step.

### Step 1 — Situate

Before you analyze the words, place the source. Note:

- **Author.** Who? What position did they speak from (clerical, commercial, governmental, anonymous)?
- **Date.** When precisely? What were they (and their audience) experiencing?
- **Genre.** Letter, sermon, treaty, novel, decree, broadside, lecture, statute, image caption?
- **Audience.** Who was meant to read or hear this? Who in fact did?
- **Medium.** Manuscript, printed book, pamphlet, newspaper, photograph, broadcast?

A surprising amount of analytic mileage comes from getting these five right. A pamphlet printed for circulation in 1520 Wittenberg behaves differently from a 1730 London periodical or a 1968 Paris broadside, and the differences are most of what is interesting.

### Step 2 — Annotate

Read with a pencil. On a printed page or PDF, mark the moves the source is making:

- Underline thesis-bearing sentences.
- Circle key terms — words the source defines or insists on.
- Bracket transitions ("but," "however," "therefore," "I conclude").
- Flag rhetorical devices: irony, hyperbole, metaphor, litotes, repetition.
- Note silences: what should be there and isn't?

Annotation is not decoration. Each mark records a question you are now obliged to answer.

### Step 3 — Analyze

Now write — in continuous sentences, not bullets — a 200- to 400-word analysis that addresses:

- **Argument.** What does the source claim? In what order does it stage its claims?
- **Method.** How does it support its claims? Authority? Evidence? Emotional appeal? Anecdote? Statistics?
- **Stakes.** What is at risk for the author or audience? What does the text want to accomplish?
- **Tensions.** Where does the source contradict itself or strain to harmonize incompatible claims?

This is the paragraph that counts on an exam.

### Step 4 — Situate again, harder

Return to context with what you have learned. Ask:

- How does this source fit — or not fit — the historiographical narrative we have studied?
- What does it confirm? What does it complicate?
- What kind of source would need to corroborate or refute it?

This step is what distinguishes A-level work from competent summary.

## Worked example 1 — Burckhardt on the Renaissance state

Open {{FILE:wikimedia-burckhardt-renaissance-italy-pdf}} and read the opening of Part I, "The State as a Work of Art."

**Situate.** Jacob Burckhardt (1818–1897), Swiss historian, professor at Basel; *Die Cultur der Renaissance in Italien* (1860); a learned synthesis aimed at educated German-reading bourgeois; printed monograph in the era of the rising university discipline of *Geschichtswissenschaft*.

**Annotate.** Note the metaphor in the section title — the *state* as an *artwork* — and the recurring vocabulary of "individual," "calculation," "freedom from tradition." The text positions Renaissance Italy as the birthplace of "modern" political consciousness.

**Analyze.** Burckhardt's claim is that fifteenth-century Italy invented the secular, calculating, self-conscious state. His method is largely typological: he assembles examples (Florence, Milan, Venice) under abstract headings rather than narrating chronologically. The stakes are interpretive — Burckhardt wants the Renaissance to be the dawn of the modern, against the medieval past — and political: a nineteenth-century liberal sees in fifteenth-century Italian self-fashioning the genealogy of his own civic ideals. The tension worth flagging is that Burckhardt's "individuals" are almost all male, almost all elite, and almost all Florentine; the typology he builds quietly excludes most of the population it claims to characterize.

**Situate again.** Subsequent scholarship — from Hans Baron's civic humanism to feminist revisions in the 1980s — has corrected, qualified, or rejected Burckhardt at almost every point. Yet the categories he supplied ("individualism," "discovery of the world and of man") remain the ones we still argue against, which is itself an index of his importance. ## Worked example 2 — A literary primary source

Close reading is not only for treaties and pamphlets. Open {{FILE:gutenberg-huckleberry-finn-txt}}. Read the opening paragraph of Chapter 1.

**Situate.** Mark Twain (Samuel Clemens), American, 1885; serial publication; aimed at a general adult readership though marketed partly as a "boy's book"; a novel composed two decades after the Civil War, set two decades before it.

**Annotate.** Note the first-person voice ("You don't know about me without you have read a book by the name of *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer*"), the meta-textual gesture (a fictional narrator referring to the author by name), and the linguistic register — vernacular American English at a moment when literary English was still expected to be formal.

**Analyze.** The opening simultaneously installs a first-person narrator who is socially marginal (Huck), establishes a literary register that signals sympathy with vernacular speech, and binds the novel to the earlier book in the series. The stakes are aesthetic and political: vernacular voice is a wager on whose speech counts as literature. Read against the post-Reconstruction context of 1885, the novel's racial politics — Jim's depiction, the use of period racial slurs — become available as historical evidence about late-nineteenth-century American attitudes, not just about the antebellum world the novel claims to depict.

**Situate again.** *Huckleberry Finn* is itself part of the cultural history of the United States — its inclusion or exclusion from school curricula has been a recurring controversy since the early twentieth century, an argument the novel anticipates and to some degree provokes. A close reading that sees only Huck and Jim and not the publication context misses what the source can teach.

## Common errors to avoid

- **Plot summary masquerading as analysis.** Tell us what the source is *doing*, not what it *says*.
- **Decontextualized quotation.** Always introduce a quotation with author, date, and context; always interpret afterward.
- **Anachronistic vocabulary.** Don't describe Erasmus as "secular" or Voltaire as "woke." Use period-appropriate categories or flag the anachronism explicitly.
- **The "obviously" trap.** If something is obvious it does not need saying; if it does need saying it is not obvious.

## Practice routine

Pick one primary source per week from the unit you are currently in. Run the four-step routine. Save the analyses; by midterm you will have six, and the patterns in your own thinking will start to emerge.

When you return to a source for a second pass — exam preparation, paper revision — do the routine again from scratch rather than re-reading your earlier annotations. The point is the practice, not the artifact.
