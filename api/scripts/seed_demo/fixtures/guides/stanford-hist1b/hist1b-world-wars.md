# The World Wars

The two world wars are best treated, as Eric Hobsbawm and others have argued, as a single thirty-one-year European civil war (1914–1945) interrupted by an unstable peace. Reading them as one episode makes the chronology tractable: an industrialized continent in 1914, a fractured continent in 1919, an authoritarian-leaning continent in the 1930s, a devastated continent in 1945, and — by 1949 — a continent partitioned by a bipolar superpower system that the wars themselves had created.

This unit takes the wars as connected, traces the interwar mechanisms that linked them, and ends with the long shadow they cast over the politics of {{GUIDE:hist1b-twentieth-century-movements}}.

## The July Crisis and the slide into 1914

On 28 June 1914 a Bosnian Serb nationalist, Gavrilo Princip, assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary in Sarajevo. Within five weeks five great powers were at war. The historiography of why is enormous; for HIST 1B four causal layers matter:

1. **Alliance system.** Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) versus Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain) meant that a Balkan crisis could not stay Balkan.
2. **Mobilization timetables.** German planning (the Schlieffen-Moltke plan) required defeating France quickly before Russia could mobilize. Once Russia ordered general mobilization on 30 July, German military logic drove diplomatic logic.
3. **Imperial competition.** Anglo-German naval rivalry, French colonial recovery after 1870, Russian Pan-Slavism — overlapping imperial ambitions thickened the Balkan crisis.
4. **Political cultures.** Mass nationalism, militarized civic ritual, and a press cycle that rewarded brinksmanship lowered the threshold for war.

Christopher Clark's *The Sleepwalkers* (2012) is the influential recent reframing, emphasizing the contingent diplomatic missteps over deep structural causes. Margaret MacMillan's *The War That Ended Peace* (2013) gives a complementary account.

## 1914–1918: industrial war

The opening campaigns of 1914 expected mobility and got industrial slaughter. The Western Front stabilized into 700 km of trenches by November 1914 and barely moved for three and a half years. Verdun (February–December 1916) cost roughly 700,000 casualties for negligible territorial gain; the Somme (July–November 1916) cost over a million. The Eastern Front was more mobile but no less lethal; the Italian, Ottoman, and African theaters extended the war globally.

Three innovations of 1914–18 set the template for twentieth-century warfare:

- **Industrial mobilization.** Munitions ministries, women's labor in war industries, conscription on an unprecedented scale.
- **Military technology.** Machine gun, barbed wire, and field artillery on the defense; tank, aircraft, and infiltration tactics by 1918 on the offense.
- **Total war as a political concept.** Civilian populations were targets — by blockade (Britain against Germany) and by deliberate atrocity (the Armenian Genocide of 1915–1916, in which roughly one million Armenians were killed by the Ottoman state).

The Russian Empire collapsed in February 1917, the United States entered in April 1917, and the German spring offensives of 1918 failed by the summer. Armistice came on 11 November 1918. Estimates of military deaths run to roughly 9–10 million; civilian deaths from war-related famine, disease, and the 1918–19 influenza pandemic added many millions more.

## The interwar settlement (and its failure)

The Treaty of Versailles (28 June 1919) imposed reparations on Germany, transferred German colonial territory to League of Nations mandates, and (by Article 231) assigned Germany sole responsibility for the war. The treaty was a compromise that satisfied none of its principal authors. Wilson lost the U.S. Senate; Clemenceau judged the German concessions insufficient; the new German republic resented the terms as a *Diktat*.

The interwar order failed in three roughly sequential ways:

1. **Economic.** Reparations fed back into German hyperinflation (1923), American capital flows propped up the system (Dawes Plan 1924, Young Plan 1929), and the 1929 Wall Street crash detonated the whole structure. By 1932 industrial production in Germany and the United States was down nearly 50 percent from 1929 levels.
2. **Political.** Liberal-democratic regimes either fell (Italy in 1922, Germany in 1933, Spain by 1939) or survived under serious strain (France with the Popular Front, Britain through the National Government). The Soviet Union, having survived its own civil war (1918–22), became under Stalin from 1929 onward a centralized command economy that combined rapid industrialization with mass terror (collectivization, 1929–33; Great Terror, 1936–38).
3. **Diplomatic.** The League of Nations failed to enforce its own collective-security principles — Japan's invasion of Manchuria (1931), Italy's invasion of Ethiopia (1935), Germany's remilitarization of the Rhineland (1936), the Spanish Civil War (1936–39), the Anschluss (1938), Munich (1938), the Molotov–Ribbentrop Pact (August 1939). Germany invaded Poland on 1 September 1939; Britain and France declared war on 3 September.

## 1939–1945: catastrophe at scale

The Second World War surpassed the First in every dimension that matters. Estimates of total deaths run to 60–75 million, the majority civilian. The European war saw three connected catastrophes:

- **The Eastern Front (1941–45).** Operation Barbarossa (22 June 1941) opened a war of annihilation; Soviet military and civilian losses exceeded 25 million. Stalingrad (winter 1942–43) and Kursk (July 1943) turned the eastern campaign.
- **Strategic bombing.** Allied bombing of German cities (Hamburg 1943, Dresden 1945) and Axis bombing of British, Soviet, and Chinese cities killed hundreds of thousands of civilians and pioneered the doctrine that would justify the atomic attacks of August 1945.
- **The Holocaust.** Between 1941 and 1945 Nazi Germany and its collaborators murdered approximately six million European Jews — roughly two-thirds of the prewar Jewish population of Europe — alongside Roma, Soviet POWs, disabled Germans, gay men, and political opponents. The shift from persecution to industrial extermination was made explicit at the Wannsee Conference (20 January 1942). The historical literature on Nazi decision-making (Browning, Friedländer, Snyder) is extensive and remains a point of intense scholarly argument.

In the Pacific, the war ended with the U.S. atomic attacks on Hiroshima (6 August 1945) and Nagasaki (9 August 1945), followed by the Soviet declaration of war on Japan and Japan's surrender on 15 August. The Tokyo and Nuremberg tribunals (1945–48) established the legal categories of crimes against peace, war crimes, and crimes against humanity that still structure international law.

## What the wars left behind

By 1945 the European powers that had begun the century as the world's hegemonic states were militarily exhausted, financially broke, and morally compromised. The two extra-European powers that emerged were the United States and the Soviet Union. Within four years their rivalry produced the Berlin Blockade (1948–49), NATO (1949), the German partition (1949), and a bipolar global system whose European core would not be redrawn until 1989.
