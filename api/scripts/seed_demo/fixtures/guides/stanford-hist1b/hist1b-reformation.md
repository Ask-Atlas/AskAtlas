# The Reformation

The Reformation is the rare historical event that announces itself with a date. On 31 October 1517, an Augustinian friar named Martin Luther (1483–1546) is said to have nailed his Ninety-five Theses to the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg. Whether the nails were literal or metaphorical, the document reached print within weeks and spread across the Holy Roman Empire within months. By 1521 Luther had been excommunicated by Leo X and outlawed by Charles V at the Diet of Worms; by 1530 the Augsburg Confession had codified a separate Lutheran church; by 1648 the Peace of Westphalia recognized Calvinist, Lutheran, and Catholic confessional states as juridically coequal. A century and a quarter is a short time to remake the political map of a continent.

This unit treats the Reformation as three braided processes — a theological argument about salvation, a media event powered by the printing press, and a political reorganization driven by princes who saw confessional choice as a tool of statecraft. Hold all three in view; reduce the Reformation to any one and the rest of the century stops making sense.

## The theological argument

Luther's break with Rome was, on his own telling, the resolution of a personal anxiety: how could a sinner stand before a righteous God? His answer — *sola fide*, justification by faith alone — read Paul (especially Romans 1:17, "the just shall live by faith") through Augustine and against the late-medieval penitential system. If salvation is a gift received by faith, then indulgences (the immediate occasion of the Ninety-five Theses, sold by Johann Tetzel to fund St Peter's in Rome) are at best superfluous and at worst a fraud on the laity.

From *sola fide* unfolded the rest of what we now call the Lutheran "solas":

- ***sola scriptura*** — Scripture, not church tradition, is the final authority. This required vernacular Bibles. Luther's German New Testament (Wittenberg, 1522) and complete Bible (1534) became, simultaneously, a religious settlement and a foundational text of the German language.
- ***sola gratia*** — grace, not works, is the means of salvation.
- **Priesthood of all believers** — every baptized Christian has direct access to God; the sacramental priesthood is, in principle, dissolved.

A second wave of reformers radicalized further. Huldrych Zwingli in Zürich (1484–1531) read the Eucharist as memorial rather than real presence. John Calvin, in Geneva from 1536, systematized Reformed theology in the *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (first edition 1536, definitive 1559) and added the doctrine of double predestination — God has eternally elected some to salvation and others to damnation, a teaching that gave Calvinist communities both their famous moral discipline and their famous capacity for political resistance.

> "We call predestination God's eternal decree, by which he compacted with himself what he willed to become of each man." — Calvin, *Institutes* III.21.5

## The print event

The Reformation is the first European event whose pace was set by movable type. Gutenberg's press had existed since the 1450s, but in the half-century between 1517 and the late 1560s the volume and speed of religious publication exploded:

- Luther's *Sermon on Indulgences and Grace* (1518) ran through 25 editions in two years.
- Between 1518 and 1525 Wittenberg presses produced roughly one Luther title every two weeks.
- Vernacular pamphlets — *Flugschriften* — short, illustrated, and cheap, carried the controversy to readers and to those who heard pamphlets read aloud.

Print did not "cause" the Reformation, but it changed what theological argument could be: a public, fast-moving controversy that princes had to take a position on. The Catholic side caught up — the Council of Trent's decrees were themselves printed and circulated — but for the first decade Luther held a media advantage that no Pope had had to think about before.

## The political reorganization

The Reformation could not have survived without princely protection. Frederick the Wise of Saxony shielded Luther after Worms; the Schmalkaldic League (1531) coordinated Lutheran territorial defense. The result was a new kind of European politics, codified in two settlements:

- **Peace of Augsburg (1555)** — *cuius regio, eius religio*: each prince of the Holy Roman Empire chose Lutheran or Catholic for his territory, and subjects could emigrate if they disagreed. Calvinism was excluded.
- **Peace of Westphalia (1648)** — ended the Thirty Years' War; extended toleration to Calvinism; effectively ended the Empire as a unified confessional polity.

Between these two settlements lay the wars of religion: the French Wars (1562–1598, ending in the Edict of Nantes), the Dutch Revolt (1568–1648), and the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648). Demographic estimates for the Empire during the last of these run as high as 30–40 percent population loss in some regions. Confessional difference, mobilized by states, killed on a scale not seen again in Europe until the twentieth century.

## The Catholic Reformation

Older textbooks called this the "Counter-Reformation," as if Catholic renewal were merely reactive. Recent scholarship prefers "Catholic Reformation" to capture the autonomous reform impulses that pre-dated and ran alongside Luther: the *Oratory of Divine Love* in Italy (founded c. 1497), Cardinal Cisneros's reforms in Spain, the founding of the Society of Jesus by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540. The **Council of Trent** (1545–1563, three sessions) hardened doctrine on justification (against Luther), reaffirmed seven sacraments, and standardized clerical training through diocesan seminaries — a Tridentine settlement whose pastoral architecture survived into the twentieth century.

## What this changes for HIST 1B

The Reformation ends the unitary Latin Christendom that the Renaissance still assumed and that {{GUIDE:hist1b-renaissance-humanism}} took as its background. Three downstream consequences shape every later unit:

1. **Confessional states.** Religion becomes an attribute of polity. The early-modern state acquires the habit of regulating belief.
2. **Vernacular print publics.** Reading congregations turn into reading publics. By the time of the Enlightenment they will sustain newspapers, periodicals, and the public sphere.
3. **Doctrinal pluralism as a problem to be managed.** Toleration, conscience, and the limits of state power become live questions — questions that Locke, Bayle, and Voltaire will inherit in {{GUIDE:hist1b-enlightenment-thought}}.
