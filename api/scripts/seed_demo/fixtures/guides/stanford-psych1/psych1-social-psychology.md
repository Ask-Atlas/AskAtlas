# Social Psychology

> Social psychology studies how the real, imagined, or implied
> presence of others changes what people think, feel, and do.

That definition does a lot of work. *Imagined* is in there because
people behave differently when they're alone but think they're being
observed. *Implied* is in there because written rules and norms shape
behaviour even with no one in the room. Most of what's distinctive
about human cognition only happens because we're a relentlessly social
species.

## Attribution: explaining behaviour

When you watch someone do something, you immediately ask: *why?* The
answer you give is an **attribution**, and it falls into two broad
categories:

- **Dispositional** (internal) — they did it because of who they are.
- **Situational** (external) — they did it because of what they were
  facing.

Two findings dominate this section.

**Fundamental attribution error.** We systematically over-weight
disposition and under-weight situation, especially when explaining
*other people's* behaviour. The driver who cut you off is reckless;
when you cut someone off, it's because you're late and the merge
was confusing.

**Actor-observer asymmetry.** We tend to make dispositional
attributions for *others* and situational ones for *ourselves* — same
behaviour, different explanation. Part of this is informational
(you have access to your own situation, not the other person's), part
is motivational.

A related bias: the **self-serving bias**, attributing successes to
ourselves and failures to circumstance. Standard issue across
cultures, though weaker in some East Asian samples.

## Conformity, compliance, obedience

Three classical paradigms map the spectrum of social influence.

**Asch line studies (1951) — conformity.** Participants judged which
of three comparison lines matched a target line. With confederates
giving an obviously wrong answer first, about a third of trials had
the real participant conforming, even on perceptually trivial
judgements. Why? Two pressures — *normative* (wanting to fit in) and
*informational* (assuming the group must know something you don't).
Conformity dropped sharply when even one confederate dissented.

**Milgram obedience studies (1961-).** Participants were instructed by
an experimenter in a lab coat to deliver increasingly severe "shocks"
to a confederate "learner." About 65% delivered the maximum 450-volt
shock. The findings shocked the field and contributed to the modern
research-ethics regime ({{GUIDE:psych1-research-methods}}). Modern
partial replications (Burger 2009) reproduce qualitatively similar
obedience rates within ethical constraints.

**Zimbardo Stanford Prison Experiment (1971).** College students
randomly assigned to "guard" or "prisoner" roles in a basement mock
prison; the study was halted early after escalating abuse. The
canonical interpretation — situations powerfully shape behaviour —
has held up better than the experimental rigour, which has been
seriously challenged in the modern reanalyses.

> The shared lesson of these three studies is not "people are bad."
> It's that ordinary people in unusual situations behave in ways they
> wouldn't predict for themselves. Situations matter.

## Attitudes and persuasion

An **attitude** is an evaluation — positive or negative — of a person,
object, or idea, with cognitive, affective, and behavioural components.
Two attitude effects are essential PSYCH 1 vocabulary:

**Cognitive dissonance** (Festinger). Holding two inconsistent
cognitions creates aversive arousal that we resolve by changing one
of them. The classic Festinger & Carlsmith (1959) study paid
participants $1 or $20 to lie about how interesting a boring task
was. Counterintuitively, the *$1* group later rated the task as more
enjoyable — they had less external justification for lying, so they
adjusted their attitude to match. The principle generalises broadly:
behaviour drags attitude after it.

**Elaboration likelihood model** of persuasion (Petty & Cacioppo).
Two routes:

- **Central route** — careful evaluation of the argument's content.
  Used when people are motivated and able to think hard. Produces
  more durable attitude change.
- **Peripheral route** — superficial cues (attractive source, simple
  heuristics, mood). Used when motivation or capacity is low.
  Produces more transient change.

Advertising design implicitly chooses one route; political
communication often targets both simultaneously.

## Group behaviour

When people act in groups, several reliable phenomena emerge:

- **Social facilitation** — performance on simple, well-learned tasks
  *improves* in the presence of others; performance on novel or
  complex tasks *worsens*. The arousal that an audience supplies helps
  the dominant response, whatever that response is.
- **Social loafing** — individual effort drops when contributions are
  pooled and individually unidentifiable. The tug-of-war
  demonstrations are the classic; the same effect appears in group
  projects.
- **Deindividuation** — anonymity and group immersion reduce
  self-awareness and inhibition; aggressive or norm-breaking behaviour
  becomes more likely. Online flame wars and crowd violence both
  exploit this.
- **Group polarisation** — group discussion among like-minded people
  shifts the average view *toward* the extreme, not the centre.
- **Groupthink** — cohesive groups pursuing consensus suppress
  dissent and arrive at worse decisions than the members would have
  individually. Janis's analyses of the Bay of Pigs and the Challenger
  disaster are the canonical case studies.

## Prejudice and discrimination

A **stereotype** is a cognitive schema about a group; **prejudice** is
the affective evaluation; **discrimination** is the behaviour.
Stereotypes can be activated automatically without endorsement —
documented via the Implicit Association Test and similar paradigms —
and can influence judgement even in people who consciously reject
them.

Two robust reduction strategies:

- **Contact hypothesis** (Allport) — sustained, equal-status contact
  between groups, in pursuit of common goals, reduces prejudice. The
  conditions matter; mere proximity isn't enough.
- **Superordinate goals** — Sherif's Robbers Cave experiment showed
  that two groups in conflict could be brought together by tasks
  that required cooperation (a broken water supply they had to fix
  jointly).

## Helping and aggression

The **bystander effect** — the more people present, the less likely
any one of them is to help — was studied after the Kitty Genovese
case (whose details have been challenged, but the laboratory effect
itself is robust). The mechanism is **diffusion of responsibility**:
each onlooker assumes someone else will act. Naming a specific person
("you, in the red shirt — call 911") reliably breaks the diffusion.

Aggression has a heritability component, hormonal correlates
(testosterone, modestly), situational triggers (frustration,
provocation, heat), and learned components (Bandura's Bobo doll
studies are again relevant — see
{{GUIDE:psych1-learning-conditioning}}).

## A note on replication

Several findings in this chapter have a complicated relationship with
the modern replication literature. The Stanford Prison Experiment, the
ego-depletion model of self-control, the Macbeth-effect priming
studies, and parts of the implicit-bias literature have all been
reanalysed or partially failed to replicate at full effect size.
Bring the rubric from {{GUIDE:psych1-research-methods}} when you read
about a single dramatic social-psych result.
