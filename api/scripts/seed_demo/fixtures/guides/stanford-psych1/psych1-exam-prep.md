# Exam Prep — PSYCH 1

This guide is the exam-strategy companion to the rest of the course. It
deliberately uses the findings from {{GUIDE:psych1-memory}} on itself:
spaced practice, retrieval, deep encoding, sleep. If those phrases
don't ring a bell yet, read the memory guide first — the techniques
below will land harder.

## What the exam actually tests

Both the midterm and the final draw from four kinds of question, and
each rewards a different study habit:

1. **Vocabulary recognition** — "Which of the following best
   describes the conjunction fallacy?" Tests whether you can match a
   technical term to its definition. Rewards flashcard-style spaced
   review.
2. **Mechanism explanation** — "Describe how saltatory conduction
   speeds an action potential." Rewards being able to walk through a
   sequence in your own words.
3. **Application to a vignette** — A scenario followed by "Which
   concept best explains the participant's behaviour?" Rewards
   *transfer* — using a concept on material you haven't seen before.
4. **Methods and interpretation** — "What alternative explanation
   does this design fail to rule out?" Rewards the rubric in
   {{GUIDE:psych1-research-methods}}.

A useful diagnostic: as you review, force yourself to write *one
question of each type* for every major concept. If you can't generate
a vignette for "negative reinforcement," you don't yet understand it
the way the exam will check.

## A four-week plan

Four weeks before the exam, working backward:

- **Week -4: Inventory.** Make a list of every learning objective from
  the syllabus. For each, mark *familiar*, *shaky*, or *blank*.
- **Week -3: First pass.** Spend 80% of your time on the *blank* and
  *shaky* topics. Do retrieval practice — close the textbook and
  write what you remember, then check.
- **Week -2: Mixed practice.** Stop topic-by-topic study. Mix items
  from across the course in one session. Interleaving feels harder in
  the moment but produces better retention.
- **Week -1: Mock exam.** Sit one full-length practice exam under
  realistic timing. Grade strictly. Whatever you missed becomes your
  focus for the last three days.

> Cramming in week -4 is a waste of cramming. The forgetting curve
> means you'll re-forget that material before the exam. Cram in
> week 0.

## The diagnostic glossary

The {{FILE:stanford-psych1-diagnostic-glossary}} document is a
flashcard-style list of every term the course expects you to recognise
on sight. Run it as spaced retrieval, not as re-reading. A simple
algorithm that works:

1. Read the term, hide the definition.
2. Try to produce the definition in your own words.
3. Reveal. If correct, mark it for a longer interval (24h, 3d, 7d).
   If wrong, mark for the same day.

Three rounds and any term you've seen will be in long-term memory.

## Topic-by-topic priorities

The major chapters and the ones that historically get the most exam
weight:

- **{{GUIDE:psych1-research-methods}}** — high yield. Every
  application question implicitly tests methods literacy. Drill the
  IV/DV/operationalisation vocabulary and the difference between
  experimental and correlational designs.
- **{{GUIDE:psych1-neuroscience-primer}}** — moderate yield, but
  diagrams matter. Be able to draw a labelled neuron and the four
  cortical lobes from memory.
- **{{GUIDE:psych1-sensation-and-perception}}** — moderate yield. The
  rod/cone split, the dorsal/ventral pathways, and the Gestalt
  principles tend to recur.
- **{{GUIDE:psych1-learning-conditioning}}** — high yield. The Skinner
  2×2 grid is on essentially every exam. Make sure you're not the
  student who calls negative reinforcement "punishment."
- **{{GUIDE:psych1-memory}}** — high yield. The
  explicit/implicit split and the encoding/storage/retrieval framework
  are exam staples. The misinformation effect shows up in vignette
  form.
- **{{GUIDE:psych1-social-psychology}}** — moderate yield. Asch /
  Milgram / Festinger get the most airtime; bystander effect and
  attribution biases are perennial vignette material.

The slide deck at {{FILE:stanford-psych1-emotion-motivation-slides}}
covers the emotion-and-motivation unit (drive theory, two-factor
theory, Maslow's hierarchy, basic emotions); use it as a self-contained
review for that unit. The book design reference at

how to format your own one-page summary sheet for last-day review.

## Two days before

Two days out, switch from learning-new-material to consolidation.

- Take a second mock exam under timing.
- Sleep at least seven hours both nights. The consolidation work your
  brain does during slow-wave sleep is part of what you've been
  studying *for*.
- Caffeine is fine; sleep deprivation isn't.
- Re-read your own one-page summary sheet (the only thing in your
  hand-written notes you should be re-reading at this point).

## During the exam

A few small habits help:

- **Read the question twice before looking at the options.** You're
  much more likely to be primed by an attractive distractor on a
  hasty first read.
- **Watch for absolutes.** "Always" and "never" are usually wrong in
  psychology because most effects are conditional.
- **Eliminate then commit.** On multiple choice, cross out the two
  obvious wrongs first, then choose between the remaining options.
- **For vignette questions, name the concept silently before reading
  the choices.** If your free-recall guess matches one of the options,
  it's almost always right.
- **Skip and return.** If a question stalls you for more than 90
  seconds, mark it and come back. Time pressure causes more wrong
  answers than ignorance does.

## After the exam

Whatever the result, do one diagnostic review afterward. Look at the
items you missed and *categorise* them: vocabulary I didn't know,
mechanism I couldn't explain, vignette I misclassified, methods I
fumbled. The category tells you what study habit needs adjustment for
the next exam. Tracking those categories across the term is the single
fastest way to improve.
