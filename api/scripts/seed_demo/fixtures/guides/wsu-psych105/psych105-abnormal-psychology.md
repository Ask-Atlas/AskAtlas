# Abnormal Psychology

The abnormal-psychology unit asks two questions: *what counts as a
psychological disorder*, and *what do we do about it*. Both questions
have moved a lot in the last 50 years. The PSYCH 105 version of the
material focuses on the DSM-5-TR diagnostic framework, the major
disorder categories, the diathesis-stress model of etiology, and a
few evidence-based treatment families.

## What is "abnormal"?

> A psychological disorder is a pattern of thoughts, feelings, or
> behaviors that is **deviant**, **distressing**, **dysfunctional**,
> and (sometimes added) **dangerous** — the "4 Ds".

No single criterion is sufficient. A college student who studies
14 hours a day is deviant from the norm but probably not
distressed or dysfunctional. A grieving widow may be distressed
without being disordered. The 4 Ds are a screen, not a diagnosis.

## Diagnostic frameworks

The two major systems:

- **DSM-5-TR** (American Psychiatric Association, 2022) —
  categorical diagnoses based on observable symptom criteria.
  Atheoretical: it describes, it doesn't explain.
- **ICD-11** (World Health Organization) — international system
  used for billing and epidemiology worldwide.

The DSM has changed enormously since DSM-I (1952). Homosexuality
was a disorder until 1973. DSM-5 dropped the multi-axis system and
added dimensional ratings to several categories. The DSM is a
political document as much as a scientific one — symptom checklists
are negotiated by committees and shaped by what insurance will cover.

## Major disorder families

You're responsible for recognizing the broad categories and a
canonical example of each.

### Anxiety disorders

Excessive fear or anxiety with avoidance behaviors.

- **Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)** — chronic worry across
  many domains, ≥6 months.
- **Panic Disorder** — recurrent unexpected panic attacks plus
  fear of future attacks.
- **Specific Phobia** — disproportionate fear of a specific
  object or situation.
- **Social Anxiety Disorder** — fear of social evaluation.
- **Agoraphobia** — fear of situations from which escape is
  difficult.

### Obsessive-Compulsive and related disorders

Moved out of anxiety in DSM-5.

- **OCD** — intrusive obsessions + ritualized compulsions
  performed to reduce distress.
- **Hoarding Disorder** — persistent difficulty discarding
  possessions.

### Trauma and stressor-related disorders

- **PTSD** — re-experiencing, avoidance, negative mood, and
  arousal/reactivity following exposure to trauma.
- **Acute Stress Disorder** — similar symptoms within 1 month of
  trauma.

### Mood disorders

- **Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)** — ≥5 symptoms for ≥2 weeks
  including depressed mood or anhedonia.
- **Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)** — milder but
  chronic (≥2 years).
- **Bipolar I** — at least one manic episode (often with
  depressive episodes).
- **Bipolar II** — hypomanic + depressive episodes (no full
  mania).

### Schizophrenia spectrum

Defined by **positive symptoms** (additions to normal experience —
hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech), **negative
symptoms** (subtractions — flat affect, alogia, avolition), and
cognitive symptoms.

Onset is typically late adolescence to early adulthood. The
diathesis-stress framework fits schizophrenia well: strong genetic
diathesis (~50% concordance in identical twins) + environmental
stressor (urban birth, cannabis use, prenatal infection).

### Personality disorders

Pervasive, inflexible patterns from adolescence onward. DSM-5
groups them in three clusters:

- **Cluster A (odd/eccentric)** — Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal
- **Cluster B (dramatic/erratic)** — Antisocial, Borderline,
  Histrionic, Narcissistic
- **Cluster C (anxious/fearful)** — Avoidant, Dependent,
  Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder

Distinguish OCD (intrusive thoughts and rituals; ego-dystonic)
from OCPD (perfectionism, control; ego-syntonic). The exam loves
this contrast.

### Eating disorders

- **Anorexia Nervosa** — restriction, fear of weight gain,
  distorted body image.
- **Bulimia Nervosa** — binge-purge cycles.
- **Binge Eating Disorder** — bingeing without compensatory
  purging.

### Neurodevelopmental disorders

- **ADHD** — inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity.
- **Autism Spectrum Disorder** — social-communication deficits +
  restricted, repetitive behaviors.

## The diathesis-stress model

> Disorders emerge from the interaction between a **diathesis**
> (predisposition — usually genetic, but may also be developmental
> or temperamental) and a **stress** (environmental trigger).

Neither alone is sufficient. A genetic risk for depression may
never express in the absence of major stressors; the same stressor
may be benign in someone without the diathesis. This frame
prevents the false debate between "biological" and "environmental"
explanations.

## Etiological models in brief

- **Biological** — genetics, neurochemistry (serotonin,
  dopamine, GABA), brain structure.
- **Behavioral** — disorders as learned patterns (phobias as
  conditioned fear).
- **Cognitive** — maladaptive beliefs and information processing
  (Beck's cognitive triad in depression: negative views of self,
  world, future).
- **Sociocultural** — culture shapes which symptoms cluster and
  which are pathologized; SES and discrimination raise risk.
- **Biopsychosocial** — the integrative frame the field actually
  uses.

## Treatments

Evidence-based treatments for most disorders fall in three buckets.

### Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — first-line for most
  anxiety and depressive disorders. Targets cognitive distortions
  and avoidance behaviors.
- **Exposure-based therapies** — for phobias, PTSD, OCD;
  systematic confrontation of feared stimuli.
- **Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)** — adapted CBT for
  borderline personality disorder.
- **Psychodynamic** — explores unconscious patterns; weaker but
  real evidence base.
- **Humanistic / client-centered** (Rogers) — unconditional
  positive regard, empathy, genuineness.

### Pharmacotherapy

- **SSRIs** (fluoxetine, sertraline) — first-line for depression
  and many anxiety disorders.
- **Benzodiazepines** — short-term anxiety relief; high
  dependence risk.
- **Lithium and mood stabilizers** — for bipolar disorder.
- **Antipsychotics** — for schizophrenia spectrum and acute
  bipolar mania.

### Combined and emerging

For moderate-to-severe depression, combined pharmacotherapy + CBT
typically beats either alone. Emerging treatments include
ketamine/esketamine for treatment-resistant depression and TMS
(transcranial magnetic stimulation).

## Stigma and recovery

Most people with mental disorders are not violent and do recover
or substantially improve. Stigma worsens outcomes by delaying
help-seeking. The PSYCH 105 final usually includes one question
about challenging stigma — the canonical answer is that disorder
prevalence is high (≈20% past-year), recovery is common with
treatment, and most people with a diagnosis are not dangerous.

## Study cues

- If a vignette describes intrusive obsessions and ritual
  compulsions: OCD.
- If perfectionism and rigidity without obsessions: OCPD.
- If positive + negative symptoms after late-teen onset:
  schizophrenia spectrum.
- If recurrent unexpected attacks + worry about future attacks:
  panic disorder.
- If diathesis + stress is invoked, the answer is "interaction".
