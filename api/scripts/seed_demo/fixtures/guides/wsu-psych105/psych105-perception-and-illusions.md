# Perception and Illusions

You don't see the world. You see a model of the world that your
visual system constructs from messy retinal input, prior expectations,
and a stack of inference shortcuts. Perception is *not* a faithful
broadcast — it's a best guess. The PSYCH 105 perception unit is
mostly an inventory of those guesses and the rules they follow.

## Sensation vs perception

> **Sensation** — physical detection of a stimulus by sensory
> receptors (light hitting the retina, sound waves moving the
> cochlea).

> **Perception** — the brain's organization and interpretation of
> sensory input into meaningful experience.

A photoreceptor firing is sensation. Recognizing the firing pattern
as your grandmother's face is perception.

## Bottom-up and top-down processing

| Direction | What it means | Dominates when |
|---|---|---|
| Bottom-up | Building percept from raw sensory features | Stimuli are clear and novel |
| Top-down | Using prior knowledge / expectation to interpret | Stimuli are ambiguous or degraded |

The two operate together. When you read this sentence with several
typos still mostly intact, you're using top-down processing to fill
in what bottom-up couldn't decode.

## Psychophysics — the limits of detection

Two thresholds you need to know:

- **Absolute threshold** — the minimum stimulus intensity detectable
  50% of the time. Classic example: a single candle flame at 30 miles
  on a clear, dark night.
- **Difference threshold (just-noticeable difference, JND)** — the
  smallest detectable change. Governed by **Weber's law**: the JND
  is a constant *proportion* of the original stimulus, not a
  constant amount. Adding 1 lb to a 5 lb backpack feels different;
  adding 1 lb to a 50 lb pack does not.

**Signal detection theory** replaced the simple-threshold model with
a framework that separates sensitivity (`d'`) from response bias
(`β`). Two listeners can have identical hearing but different
willingness to say "yes, I heard it".

## Gestalt grouping principles

The Gestalt school showed that perception groups elements
automatically, by rules:

- **Figure–ground** — separating object from background.
- **Proximity** — nearby elements are grouped.
- **Similarity** — alike elements are grouped.
- **Closure** — we perceive complete figures even with gaps.
- **Continuity** — we prefer smooth, continuous lines.
- **Common fate** — elements moving together are grouped.

## Depth perception

The retina is two-dimensional; depth must be inferred. Two cue
families:

### Binocular cues (require both eyes)

- **Retinal disparity** — the slight difference between the two
  eyes' images. Larger disparity → closer object.
- **Convergence** — eye-muscle strain as the eyes turn inward to
  focus on close objects.

### Monocular cues (work with one eye)

- **Relative size** — known-similar objects appear smaller when
  farther.
- **Linear perspective** — parallel lines converge in the distance.
- **Interposition** — closer objects occlude farther ones.
- **Texture gradient** — texture density increases with distance.
- **Light and shadow** — shading reveals 3D form.
- **Motion parallax** — closer objects appear to move faster than
  farther ones during head motion.

Pictures use only monocular cues. That's why a Renaissance perspective
painting reads as 3D from one eye.

## Perceptual constancies

Despite changing retinal input, you perceive stable objects:

- **Size constancy** — your friend isn't shrinking when she walks
  away.
- **Shape constancy** — the door is rectangular even when you see
  it from an angle as a trapezoid.
- **Color constancy** — your white shirt looks white indoors and
  outdoors despite very different illuminants.
- **Brightness constancy** — perceived lightness is stable across
  different illumination levels.

Constancies are inferences. When the inference machinery is fed
unusual input, it produces illusions.

## Classic illusions and what they reveal

- **Müller-Lyer** — two equal lines look unequal because of the
  arrowhead context. Cross-cultural work showed the illusion is
  weaker in cultures without "carpentered world" right angles —
  evidence that perceptual inference is shaped by experience.
- **Ponzo** — converging lines (linear perspective) make the
  upper bar look longer.
- **Moon illusion** — the moon looks larger near the horizon than
  overhead, despite identical retinal size.
- **Ames room** — a distorted room creates apparent size
  differences when viewed through a peephole.
- **Hollow-face illusion** — a concave face mask is perceived as
  convex because faces are *always* convex in experience. A clean
  demonstration that priors override stimulus.
- **Checker shadow** (Adelson) — two squares with identical
  retinal luminance look very different because of inferred
  shadow.

## Attention and perception

You don't perceive everything in your sensory field — only what
attention lets through:

- **Selective attention** — the cocktail party effect: tracking one
  conversation in a noisy room.
- **Inattentional blindness** — Simons & Chabris's invisible
  gorilla. When attention is on counting basketball passes, half
  of viewers miss a person in a gorilla suit walking through.
- **Change blindness** — large changes between scenes go unnoticed
  if the change spans a saccade, blink, or visual disruption.

These findings undercut the idea of perception as a passive,
high-fidelity broadcast.

## Other senses, briefly

- **Hearing** — frequency → pitch (place + temporal coding);
  amplitude → loudness.
- **Touch** — pressure, temperature, pain (gate control theory).
- **Taste and smell** — chemical senses; the only senses that
  bypass the thalamus.
- **Proprioception and vestibular** — body-position and balance.

The exam is heavy on vision but expect at least one cross-modal
question, often about the McGurk effect (visual mouth shape
overrides auditory phoneme).

## Study cues

- If a question describes filling in missing details, it's top-down
  / closure.
- If a question describes a one-eye depth cue, it's monocular.
- If a question describes a two-eye depth cue, it's binocular
  (disparity or convergence).
- If a question describes Weber's law, the answer is about
  *proportional* change.
- If a question describes overlooking an obvious change, that's
  change blindness or inattentional blindness.
