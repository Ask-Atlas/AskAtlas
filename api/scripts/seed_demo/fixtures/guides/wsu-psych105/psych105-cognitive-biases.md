# Cognitive Biases and Heuristics

Human reasoning is fast, frugal, and frequently wrong. Decades of
research from Tversky, Kahneman, and others showed that the same
mental shortcuts that let you decide what to eat for lunch in three
seconds also produce predictable, systematic errors in judgment. The
PSYCH 105 unit on cognitive biases is partly a vocabulary list and
partly an exercise in noticing your own thinking.

## Two systems

> **System 1** — fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional. Always on.
> Cheap. Good at pattern matching, terrible at probability.

> **System 2** — slow, effortful, analytic. Engaged only when needed.
> Expensive. Capable of careful reasoning, but lazy and easily
> displaced by System 1.

Most everyday cognition runs on System 1. System 2 monitors and can
override, but it tires and defers. The biases below are mostly
System 1 outputs that System 2 failed to catch.

## Heuristics — the engines under the biases

Three heuristics from Tversky and Kahneman explain a large fraction
of judgment errors.

### Availability heuristic

Judging the frequency or probability of an event by how easily
examples come to mind.

- After a plane crash on the news, people overestimate flight risk.
- Vivid causes of death (homicide, shark attacks) are overestimated;
  banal causes (heart disease) underestimated.
- If you can recall it quickly, your brain treats it as common.

### Representativeness heuristic

Judging probability by how closely something resembles a prototype,
ignoring base rates.

- "Linda is 31, single, outspoken, and was a philosophy major
  involved in social justice. Is Linda more likely to be (a) a
  bank teller, or (b) a bank teller and active in the feminist
  movement?" Most people pick (b), violating the **conjunction
  rule** — P(A ∧ B) ≤ P(A).
- Doctors who get a positive mammogram from a 40-year-old often
  overestimate the post-test probability of cancer because they
  ignore the low base rate.

### Anchoring and adjustment

The first number you see anchors subsequent estimates, even when
the anchor is irrelevant. Negotiators exploit this with high
opening offers; charities exploit it with high default donation
amounts.

## A working list of biases

You'll see most of these on the exam. The {{FILE:wsu-psych105-cognitive-biases-worksheet}}
worksheet has practice scenarios for each.

| Bias | One-line definition |
|---|---|
| Confirmation bias | Seeking evidence that supports your existing belief; ignoring evidence that contradicts it |
| Hindsight bias | "I knew it all along" — past events feel more predictable than they were |
| Overconfidence | Subjective certainty exceeds objective accuracy |
| Belief perseverance | Initial beliefs survive even when their evidentiary basis is discredited |
| Framing effect | Logically equivalent options elicit different choices based on wording (90% lean vs 10% fat) |
| Loss aversion | Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains |
| Sunk cost fallacy | Past, non-recoverable costs influence current decisions |
| Endowment effect | Owning something raises its perceived value |
| Status quo bias | Default options are disproportionately chosen |
| Bandwagon | Believing or doing what others do |
| Self-serving bias | Attributing success to oneself, failure to circumstances |
| Fundamental attribution error | Over-weighting disposition vs situation when explaining others' behavior |
| Actor-observer bias | The fundamental attribution error, but only when explaining *others'* behavior — your own gets situational explanations |
| Dunning-Kruger | Low-skill individuals overestimate their skill; high-skill ones often underestimate |
| Halo effect | Positive impression in one domain bleeds into unrelated domains |

## The conjunction fallacy in detail

The Linda problem is the cleanest demonstration of a probability
violation. P(feminist bank teller) ≤ P(bank teller), always — the
intersection cannot be more probable than either set alone. The
representativeness heuristic produces the error: Linda *resembles*
the prototype "feminist" much more than she resembles "bank teller",
so the conjunction feels more representative.

Notice that this isn't a math error. It's a cognitive shortcut
substituting "how representative" for "how probable" without you
noticing the swap.

## Framing and prospect theory

Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory has two key claims:

1. **Reference dependence** — outcomes are evaluated as gains or
   losses relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms.
2. **Loss aversion** — losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent
   gains feel good.

This produces the classic Asian disease problem:

> 600 lives are at risk. Program A saves 200 for sure. Program B has
> a 1/3 chance of saving 600 and a 2/3 chance of saving none.
> *Most pick A.*

> 600 lives are at risk. Program A: 400 die for sure. Program B:
> 1/3 chance no one dies, 2/3 chance 600 die.
> *Most pick B.*

These are the same options, framed differently. The "lives saved"
frame triggers risk aversion (sure gain). The "deaths" frame
triggers risk seeking (avoid sure loss).

## Why biases persist

If biases produce errors, why does evolution let them stick around?
Three hypotheses you should know:

1. **Ecological rationality** — heuristics are adaptive in the
   environments they evolved for. Availability is a great
   frequency estimator when your sample comes from your own
   neighborhood.
2. **Cost-benefit** — System 2 is metabolically expensive.
   Biases are the cost of speed.
3. **Social signaling** — some biases (overconfidence,
   self-serving) function socially even when they're inaccurate.

## Debiasing — what actually helps

Most "debiasing" interventions fail. A few have evidence:

- **Pre-mortem** — before deciding, imagine the decision failed and
  reason backward about why. Counters overconfidence and
  confirmation bias.
- **Outside view** — base estimates on reference-class statistics,
  not narrative inside-view reasoning.
- **Consider the opposite** — explicitly generate reasons your
  current belief might be wrong.
- **Decision checklists** — codify the questions System 2 should
  ask but usually skips.

Note that *learning about a bias* doesn't immunize you against it.
You'll commit anchoring errors right after reading this section.
The shortcuts are below the level of conscious control.

## Study cues

- If a question describes underestimating base rates, it's
  representativeness.
- If a question describes a vivid example warping risk estimates,
  it's availability.
- If a question shows the same outcome producing different choices
  via wording, it's framing.
- If a question describes ignoring a sunk cost would be rational
  but isn't — sunk cost fallacy.
