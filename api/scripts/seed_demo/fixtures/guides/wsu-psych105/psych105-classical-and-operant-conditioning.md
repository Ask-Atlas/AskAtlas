# Classical and Operant Conditioning

The behaviorist program promised that if you knew the stimuli and the
consequences, you could predict and shape behavior — no need to peek
inside the mind. Pure behaviorism is out of fashion, but the two
mechanisms it described still account for an enormous slice of
day-to-day learning, from why your stomach lurches when you hear the
dental-office music to why the slot machine has lights and bells.

This guide covers both engines, the vocabulary you have to memorize,
and the distinctions that PSYCH 105 exam questions love to blur.

## Classical conditioning — Pavlov's setup

Pavlov noticed his dogs were salivating before food arrived — at the
sound of the lab assistant's footsteps. He turned that observation
into the canonical experiment.

> **Classical conditioning** — a previously neutral stimulus comes to
> elicit a response after being repeatedly paired with a stimulus that
> already elicits that response.

The standard four-term vocabulary:

| Term | Pavlov example | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| Unconditioned Stimulus (US) | Food | Naturally elicits the response |
| Unconditioned Response (UR) | Salivation to food | Reflexive, unlearned |
| Conditioned Stimulus (CS) | Bell | Previously neutral, now signals the US |
| Conditioned Response (CR) | Salivation to bell | Learned response to the CS |

Memorize this. On the exam, you'll be handed a scenario and asked to
label each element. The trap is that the CR and the UR look the same
behaviorally — what distinguishes them is *what triggered them*.

### Acquisition, extinction, spontaneous recovery

- **Acquisition** — the CS-US pairing builds up the association.
  Strongest when the CS reliably *predicts* the US (forward
  conditioning), with a short delay.
- **Extinction** — present the CS without the US repeatedly; the CR
  weakens.
- **Spontaneous recovery** — after extinction and a rest period, the
  CR can reappear briefly. Extinction doesn't erase learning; it
  inhibits it.
- **Generalization** — similar stimuli evoke the CR (a different
  bell tone also elicits salivation).
- **Discrimination** — the organism learns that only the original CS
  predicts the US.

### Real-world classical conditioning

- **Conditioned taste aversion** (Garcia effect): one pairing of a
  novel food with nausea can produce a lasting aversion, even when
  the nausea was caused by something else. Evidence that not all
  associations are equally learnable — biology constrains
  conditioning.
- **Drug tolerance**: environmental cues that predict drug
  administration trigger compensatory physiological responses; this
  is why overdoses spike when users inject in a novel setting.
- **Advertising**: pair your product (CS) with attractive imagery
  (US that already evokes positive affect → UR), and the product
  picks up the positive affect (CR).

## Operant conditioning — Skinner's box

Where classical conditioning links stimuli, operant conditioning
links *behavior* to *consequences*.

> **Operant conditioning** — behaviors followed by reinforcement
> become more frequent; behaviors followed by punishment become
> less frequent.

Two by two. Memorize this matrix; it is the most-tested table in
the unit.

| | Add a stimulus | Remove a stimulus |
|---|---|---|
| **Increases behavior** | Positive reinforcement | Negative reinforcement |
| **Decreases behavior** | Positive punishment | Negative punishment |

"Positive" and "negative" do **not** mean good and bad. They mean
*added* and *removed*. The most common student error is calling a
spanking "negative reinforcement" because it feels negative — it's
actually positive punishment (a stimulus is added to decrease the
behavior).

Worked examples:

- A rat presses a lever and gets a food pellet → **positive
  reinforcement**.
- A rat presses a lever and a loud noise stops → **negative
  reinforcement** (the aversive stimulus is removed; the behavior
  increases).
- A child touches a hot stove and feels pain → **positive
  punishment** (a stimulus is added; the behavior decreases).
- A teen breaks curfew and loses phone privileges → **negative
  punishment** (a stimulus is removed; the behavior decreases).

### Reinforcement schedules

How *often* you reinforce changes the resulting behavior pattern
dramatically.

| Schedule | Pattern | Real-world example |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed Ratio (FR) | Reinforce after every Nth response | Piecework pay |
| Variable Ratio (VR) | Reinforce after a variable number of responses | Slot machines, fishing |
| Fixed Interval (FI) | Reinforce first response after fixed time | Paychecks |
| Variable Interval (VI) | Reinforce first response after variable time | Checking email for a reply |

**Variable ratio produces the highest response rates and the most
extinction-resistant behavior.** That's why slot machines work and
why intermittently rewarded behaviors (like checking a phone) are
hard to break.

### Shaping and chaining

Operant procedures build complex behaviors by reinforcing
**successive approximations** to the target behavior — the
shaping technique that lets trainers teach a dolphin to leap
through a hoop without ever telling it what to do. Chaining links
multiple discrete behaviors so the completion of one cues the
next.

## Comparing the two

| Dimension | Classical | Operant |
|---|---|---|
| What's learned | S–S association | Response–consequence |
| Behavior type | Reflexive (involuntary) | Emitted (voluntary) |
| Key figure | Pavlov | Skinner, Thorndike |
| Acquisition | Pair CS with US | Reinforce or punish behavior |

The pairing-vs-consequence distinction is what the exam asks. If
the scenario describes an organism *responding* to a signal, it's
classical. If the scenario describes an organism *acting* and
then experiencing a consequence, it's operant.

## Biological and cognitive limits on conditioning

Behaviorists assumed any stimulus could be paired with any
response. Subsequent work showed otherwise:

- **Preparedness** — animals are biologically prepared to associate
  certain CS-US pairs (taste with illness, visual with shock) and
  resistant to others.
- **Latent learning** (Tolman) — rats learned a maze without
  reinforcement, then performed once reinforcement was introduced.
  Suggested cognitive maps, not just S-R chains.
- **Insight learning** (Köhler) — chimps solved problems with a
  sudden reorganization, not gradual shaping.

These findings opened the door to the cognitive revolution.
