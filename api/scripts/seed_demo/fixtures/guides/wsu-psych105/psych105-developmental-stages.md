# Developmental Stages

The developmental psychology unit packs four big stage theories into
about two weeks: Piaget on cognition, Erikson on psychosocial
identity, Ainsworth on attachment, and Kohlberg on moral reasoning.
You'll be expected to name the stages, the typical ages, and the
core conflict or capability of each. The {{FILE:wsu-psych105-dev-psych-stages-slides}}
slide deck has the canonical tables.

## Why stages?

Stage theorists argue development is **qualitatively** different at
different ages — not just more or faster of the same processes. A
five-year-old isn't a small adult; she's running cognitive software
with different operating constraints. Continuous theorists disagree
and emphasize gradual, quantitative change.

The truth is probably both: some capacities (vocabulary) grow
continuously, others (object permanence, formal reasoning) emerge
in step-changes.

## Piaget — cognitive development

Piaget watched his own children solve problems and built a four-stage
model of how thinking changes from infancy through adolescence.

| Stage | Age (rough) | Hallmark capability | Hallmark limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sensorimotor | 0–2 | Object permanence (~8 mo) | No mental representation early on |
| Preoperational | 2–7 | Symbolic thought; pretend play | Egocentrism; no conservation |
| Concrete operational | 7–11 | Conservation; logical operations on concrete objects | Trouble with abstract / hypothetical |
| Formal operational | 12+ | Hypothetical-deductive reasoning; abstract thought | Not all adults reach this consistently |

A few terms to know:

- **Schema** — a mental framework for organizing information.
- **Assimilation** — fitting new information into an existing schema
  (a toddler calls a horse a "doggy").
- **Accommodation** — modifying schemas when they don't fit (the
  toddler develops a separate "horse" schema).
- **Conservation** — the recognition that quantity stays the same
  despite changes in shape (water poured into a tall thin glass is
  the same amount).

Modern criticisms: Piaget underestimated infant cognition (clever
methods reveal object permanence earlier than 8 months) and
overestimated formal operations (many adults reason concretely most
of the time). The stages are still the canonical vocabulary for
the unit.

## Erikson — psychosocial development

Erikson covered the entire lifespan with eight conflicts. Each
conflict has a "win" and a "loss" outcome.

| Stage | Age | Conflict | Win |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0–1 | Trust vs Mistrust | Hope |
| 2 | 1–3 | Autonomy vs Shame/Doubt | Will |
| 3 | 3–6 | Initiative vs Guilt | Purpose |
| 4 | 6–11 | Industry vs Inferiority | Competence |
| 5 | 12–18 | Identity vs Role Confusion | Fidelity |
| 6 | 18–40 | Intimacy vs Isolation | Love |
| 7 | 40–65 | Generativity vs Stagnation | Care |
| 8 | 65+ | Integrity vs Despair | Wisdom |

The exam usually asks you to match a vignette ("a 16-year-old
trying out different friend groups and styles") to the stage
("Identity vs Role Confusion"). Memorize the ages and at least the
conflict label.

## Attachment — Ainsworth's Strange Situation

Ainsworth observed infant–caregiver pairs in a standardized
laboratory procedure where the caregiver leaves and returns. Three
classic styles plus one added later:

| Style | Behavior on reunion | Caregiver pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Secure | Greets caregiver, comforted, returns to play | Sensitive, responsive |
| Insecure-avoidant | Ignores or avoids caregiver | Cold, dismissive |
| Insecure-anxious (resistant) | Clings then resists; hard to soothe | Inconsistent |
| Disorganized (Main, later) | Confused, contradictory behavior | Frightening / frightened, often abuse |

Attachment style in infancy correlates with adult relationship
patterns, though it's not deterministic. Cross-cultural work shows
variation in the *prevalence* of styles (more avoidant in Germany,
more resistant in Japan) but the categories themselves replicate.

## Kohlberg — moral development

Kohlberg used moral dilemmas (most famously the **Heinz dilemma** —
should a man steal a drug to save his dying wife?) to score moral
reasoning, not the answer.

| Level | Stage | What guides judgment |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-conventional | 1: Punishment-obedience | Avoid punishment |
| | 2: Instrumental exchange | Self-interest, fair trade |
| Conventional | 3: Good boy / nice girl | Approval, social norms |
| | 4: Law and order | Authority, social rules |
| Post-conventional | 5: Social contract | Mutual benefit, individual rights |
| | 6: Universal ethical principles | Abstract justice |

Most adults reason at stage 3 or 4. Stage 6 is rare and contested.
Carol Gilligan critiqued Kohlberg for sampling only boys initially
and emphasizing a "justice" framework over an "ethic of care"; her
critique reshaped the field even though Kohlberg's stages are still
taught.

## Other developmental landmarks

A few capabilities you'll be asked about:

- **Theory of mind** — the realization that others have beliefs
  separate from one's own. Tested with the **false-belief task**
  ("Sally puts her marble in the basket and leaves; Anne moves it
  to the box. Where will Sally look?"). Typically passed around
  age 4.
- **Critical periods** — windows during which experience must
  happen for normal development (language acquisition is the
  cleanest case; first-language acquisition becomes much harder
  after puberty).
- **Temperament** — biologically based individual differences in
  reactivity (easy, difficult, slow-to-warm-up — Thomas & Chess).
- **Authoritative parenting** (Baumrind) — high warmth + high
  demands → consistently best outcomes across cultures.

## Adolescence and emerging adulthood

The PSYCH 105 unit usually highlights:

- **Synaptic pruning** continues into the mid-20s, especially in
  prefrontal cortex.
- **Risk taking** peaks in mid-adolescence, partly explained by
  asynchronous maturation: the limbic reward system matures
  earlier than the prefrontal regulator.
- **Identity status** (Marcia, extending Erikson) — exploration
  and commitment combine to yield achievement, foreclosure,
  moratorium, or diffusion.

## Aging

- **Crystallized intelligence** (vocabulary, accumulated facts) is
  stable or rises into late life.
- **Fluid intelligence** (novel problem solving, processing speed)
  declines from the 20s onward.
- **Dementia** is *not* normal aging. Alzheimer's involves
  progressive memory loss and is the most common dementia.

## Study cues

- If a vignette involves an 8-month-old not understanding
  hidden-object permanence, it's sensorimotor.
- If a vignette describes an 8-year-old who can't solve abstract
  algebra but understands conservation, it's concrete operational.
- If a vignette describes a 16-year-old experimenting with
  identity, it's Erikson Stage 5.
- If a vignette describes an infant who ignores the returning
  caregiver, it's avoidant attachment.
